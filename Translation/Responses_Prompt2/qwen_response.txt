Negotiations on the Vessel Salvage

The relentless rain battered the corrugated tin roof of the temporary command pavilion, creating a deafening symphony of metallic drumming that echoed through the damp air. Inside reigned a suffocating atmosphere, saturated with the acrid smell of cheap burnt coffee and the unmistakable stench of the estuary's silty murk. Outside, hidden in the gray gloom of the squall, the listing hull of the destroyer *Leviathan* lay hopelessly stranded on the treacherous shoals of the coastal strait. A lone tungsten lamp swung violently beneath the ceiling, casting chaotic, pendulum-like shadows on the faces of two men, locked in a dead grip of mutual incomprehension regarding the vessel salvage operation.

Alistair Vance, a man whose immaculate, bespoke trench coat seemed a deliberate affront to the surrounding destitution, stood motionless by the fogged window. His posture resembled a classical statue: rigid and steeped in hereditary authority. Opposite him, slouched in a rusted folding chair and unceremoniously propping his mud-caked boots on a scratched wooden table, sat Jax. Jax was the foreman of a motley salvage crew that was currently attempting, to no avail, to extract Vance’s multimillion-dollar asset from the murky depths.

"Listen, man," Jax muttered, aggressively chewing a bitten toothpick. "Here’s the deal: she’s stuck fast. The mud’s like glue. I’m not prying her loose with a magic wand. You want her out? We’ll have to cut her open. Simple as that. My guys are down there, in pitch darkness. Zero visibility. Total mess. Pile of bent metal, ruptured pipes, and mud thick as concrete. You can’t just waltz in and hook a cable. She’s gotta be carefully worked loose, got it? Handle it with care."

Alistair turned slowly; his face betrayed a profound, aristocratic fatigue. "Despite your primitive assessment, the proposal to resort to destructive measures stands in direct opposition to our objectives. Your colloquial description of this highly sensitive engineering task provokes my utmost irritation. The *Leviathan* is not some barge you can 'carefully wiggle' out of the silt. It is a vessel equipped with experimental quantum arrays, the sensitivity of which cannot be overstated. Even the slightest lateral deviation by a fraction of a degree can permanently misalign the optical matrices. I insist that we immediately shelve your supplementary proposal. The approach is, undoubtedly, innovative; nevertheless, it remains entirely unacceptable."

Jax snorted, tossing the toothpick onto the plank floor. "Shelve it? Bro, if we shelve it, we’ll just be sitting around twiddling our thumbs. And 'innovative'? I’m not reading sci-fi here, buddy. I’m trying to save your ass. You keep lecturing me on the situation but you’re ignoring the actual current. The river current is tearing through at ten knots. And the stray current from your blown generators? It’s going straight through the water. It’s a death trap, period."

"Such vivid hyperboles do little to further the fulfillment of our contractual obligations," Alistair replied in an icy tone. "The parameters of our agreement were clearly stipulated. Your persistent tendency to label a standard marine salvage operation a 'death trap' is a dramatic oversimplification of a hydrostatic equation that possesses a mathematical solution. I am fully aware of the hydrological and electrical realities. However, this morning, standing at the water's edge, I happened to observe a lone white heron peacefully nesting on the riverbank. It was entirely unbothered by the cacophony of that monstrous mechanical crane you have so unreliably parked nearby. Speaking of banks... my superiors at the central financial bank in London are exhibiting extraordinary impatience regarding your protracted delays."

"Yeah, right? Then tell your suits to come down here and take a stroll along that shore," Jax parried, pointing fiercely toward the window. "It’s one big swamp out there. They’ll bury their shiny Italian boots in two seconds flat. My pumps are failing, hoses are bursting, and the winch is about to blow to hell on the first bad pull. We’re running on fumes, blue electrical tape, and pure spite right now."

Jax leaned forward, dropping his boots to the floor with a heavy thud that made the coffee mugs tremble. "Listen, Alistair, we can’t put off the problem indefinitely. We have to swallow this pill right here and now. The elephant in the room is that your vessel is literally breaking in half. Let’s get to the point. You want to extract the servers from the hull, but you’re too cheap to authorize heavy lifting gear."

Alistair stiffened, adjusting his thin metal-framed glasses. "Your use of colloquial clichés does nothing to advance our shared endeavor. I have thoroughly reviewed the preliminary draft of your amended contract, as well as the schematic for the watertight container. The financial estimate, which we would be forced to draw from our operating reserves, borders on extortion, particularly given the surprisingly shallow draft of the vessel in its current deplorable condition. Moreover, standing near this cracked glass, I am enduring a rather foul draft."

"Then put on a sweater," Jax groaned, rolling his eyes toward the tin roof. "I’ve got thirty men out there freezing to death. I’m not asking them to risk drowning just so you can save a few bucks. If you don’t approve the hazard pay, I’m going to lay it all out straight to the union reps. Then they’ll just walk. They’ll wash their hands of it, man, and leave your glowing boxes to rot in the mud."

Alistair closed his eyes, pinching the bridge of his nose in a display of supreme restraint. "Must I remind you of the catastrophic failure we endured in the North Sea two years ago? Corporate management dismissed the union representatives because they were frightened by a reduction in the profit margin. Consequently, the operation proceeded amid glaring logistical miscalculations. During the extraction phase, heavy iron chains crushed the wooden support pallets, as they proved too fragile to withstand the kinetic load. I will not permit a recurrence of such staggering incompetence."

Jax shook his head, looking utterly exhausted. "Look, I don't give a damn about the North Sea. What matters is what's happening right now. Are you signing the work order or not?"

Alistair’s voice dropped to an icy, authoritative tone, heavy with bureaucratic finality. "Listen to me very carefully, Jax. If the International Maritime Tribunal determines that the structural integrity of the hull was irreversibly compromised prior to our intervention today, it is absolutely vital to shift all liability exclusively onto your syndicate, whose egregious negligence has been thoroughly and meticulously documented."

Jax stared at him blankly. "Come again?"

Alistair continued in an uncompromising tone. "The primary insurers were strictly instructed to immediately halt extraction to prevent the rupture of the Class II bulkhead; this unforeseen circumstance, had your engineering team properly forecast it, would have been entirely averted by the reinforcement protocols, which, for inexplicable reasons, were discarded."

Jax rubbed his temples, offering a smile devoid of humor. "Man, your bark is way worse than your bite. But fine. You wanna talk liability? The coating on those wooden pilings out there is stripped because your ship rammed straight into them. We didn’t do that. And don’t even get me started on the security detail you brought in; their guard dogs kept my crew awake all damn night with their endless barking."

Alistair sighed heavily; the sound was barely audible over the relentless drumming of the rain. "This body of water, this treacherous coastal strait, has proven far more formidable than our initial sonar surveys indicated. The ambient noise of the storm completely drowns out the groaning of the metal. I require nothing more than a carefully considered operational strategy, not a reckless, impulsive gamble."

"You need a carefully considered strategy?" Jax stood up, aggressively zipping his high-visibility jacket all the way to his chin. "Pay the hazard rate. Give my guys a real cut. Do that, and we’ll rig the pontoons, purge the ballast, and lift your precious cargo to the surface. Don’t, and it’ll stay on the bottom forever. Your call, boss."

Alistair gazed deeply into the gray, churning abyss beyond the window, watching the rain lash against the glass. "Although the board of directors has repeatedly proposed leaving the sunken cargo to the mercy of the unforgiving depths, I am firmly convinced: evacuation must remain our priority, provided the divers, who have been fully apprised of the hazardous conditions, are guaranteed their exorbitant compensation should the operation be catastrophically compromised."

Jax grinned, a flash of white teeth piercing the pavilion’s dim, gloomy light. "Now you’re speaking my language, man. I’ll prep the crew and have them in the water in ten."

"I sincerely doubt I will ever speak your language, Jax," Alistair murmured, turning away from the window as the salvage foreman exited the tent into the howling storm. "But let us hope, for both our sakes, that this sorry enterprise finally bears fruit."
