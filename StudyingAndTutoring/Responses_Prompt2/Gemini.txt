Here is the complete, step-by-step solution to the eigenvalue problem, followed by two practice exercises with their solutions.

---

### **Part (a): Finding the Eigenvalues**

To find the eigenvalues of the matrix $B$, we must solve the characteristic equation, which is given by setting the determinant of $(B - \lambda I)$ to zero.

**1. Set up the matrix $(B - \lambda I)$:**
Subtract the scalar $\lambda$ from the diagonal entries of the matrix $B$. The identity matrix $I$ ensures we are only subtracting $\lambda$ from the main diagonal.

$$B - \lambda I = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} \lambda & 0 \\ 0 & \lambda \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 - \lambda & 1 \\ 2 & 3 - \lambda \end{pmatrix}$$

**2. Calculate the determinant:**
For a $2 \times 2$ matrix $\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}$, the determinant is $(ad - bc)$. We apply this to our matrix:

$$\det(B - \lambda I) = (4 - \lambda)(3 - \lambda) - (1)(2)$$

**3. Expand and simplify to form the characteristic polynomial:**
Multiply the binomials and subtract the constant:

$$(12 - 4\lambda - 3\lambda + \lambda^2) - 2 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10$$

**4. Solve the characteristic equation:**
Set the polynomial to zero and factor it to find the roots (the eigenvalues). We need two numbers that multiply to $10$ and add to $-7$, which are $-5$ and $-2$.

$$\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = 0$$
$$(\lambda - 5)(\lambda - 2) = 0$$

Setting each factor to zero gives us our eigenvalues:
**$\lambda_1 = 5$** and **$\lambda_2 = 2$**.

---

### **Part (b): Finding the Eigenvectors**

For each eigenvalue, we must find a non-zero vector $\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}$ that satisfies the equation $(B - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$.

**1. Find the eigenvector for $\lambda_1 = 5$:**
Substitute $\lambda = 5$ into the matrix $(B - \lambda I)$:

$$B - 5I = \begin{pmatrix} 4 - 5 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 - 5 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 2 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Now, solve the system $\begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 2 & -2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$. This translates to the system of equations:

$$-x + y = 0$$
$$2x - 2y = 0$$

Notice that the second equation is simply a multiple of the first (which confirms we did the determinant correctly, as the matrix is singular). Using the first equation, we find:

$$-x + y = 0 \implies y = x$$

Letting $x = 1$, we get $y = 1$.
The corresponding eigenvector is **$\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$**.

**2. Find the eigenvector for $\lambda_2 = 2$:**
Substitute $\lambda = 2$ into the matrix $(B - \lambda I)$:

$$B - 2I = \begin{pmatrix} 4 - 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 - 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Now, solve the system $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$. This yields the equations:

$$2x + y = 0$$
$$2x + y = 0$$

From this, we can express $y$ in terms of $x$:

$$y = -2x$$

Letting $x = 1$, we get $y = -2$.
The corresponding eigenvector is **$\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}$**.

---

### **Part (c): Verification**

We will verify our result for $\lambda_1 = 5$ and $\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$ by confirming that $B\mathbf{v}_1 = \lambda_1\mathbf{v}_1$.

**1. Compute the left side, $B\mathbf{v}_1$:**
Multiply the original matrix $B$ by the eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_1$:

$$B\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} (4)(1) + (1)(1) \\ (2)(1) + (3)(1) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 + 1 \\ 2 + 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}$$

**2. Compute the right side, $\lambda_1\mathbf{v}_1$:**
Multiply the eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_1$ by the scalar eigenvalue $\lambda_1 = 5$:

$$\lambda_1\mathbf{v}_1 = 5\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}$$

Since $\begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}$, the equation $B\mathbf{v}_1 = \lambda_1\mathbf{v}_1$ holds true. The calculation is verified.

### **Follow-Up Exercises**

Here are two similar problems to reinforce this process, complete with step-by-step solutions.

#### **Exercise 1**
Find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors for the matrix $C = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & -2 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.

**Solution 1:**
1.  **Characteristic Equation:**
    $$\det(C - \lambda I) = \det\begin{pmatrix} 5 - \lambda & -2 \\ 1 & 2 - \lambda \end{pmatrix} = 0$$
    $$(5 - \lambda)(2 - \lambda) - (-2)(1) = 0$$
    $$10 - 5\lambda - 2\lambda + \lambda^2 + 2 = 0$$
    $$\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 12 = 0$$
    $$(\lambda - 3)(\lambda - 4) = 0$$
    The eigenvalues are **$\lambda_1 = 3$** and **$\lambda_2 = 4$**.
2.  **Eigenvector for $\lambda_1 = 3$:**
    $$C - 3I = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -2 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$$
    Solving $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -2 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ yields $x - y = 0$, so $x = y$.
    Let $x = 1$, then $y = 1$. The eigenvector is **$\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$**.
3.  **Eigenvector for $\lambda_2 = 4$:**
    $$C - 4I = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$$
    Solving $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & -2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ yields $x - 2y = 0$, so $x = 2y$.
    Let $y = 1$, then $x = 2$. The eigenvector is **$\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$**.

#### **Exercise 2**
Find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors for the matrix $D = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$.

**Solution 2:**
1.  **Characteristic Equation:**
    $$\det(D - \lambda I) = \det\begin{pmatrix} 1 - \lambda & 4 \\ 2 & 3 - \lambda \end{pmatrix} = 0$$
    $$(1 - \lambda)(3 - \lambda) - (4)(2) = 0$$
    $$3 - \lambda - 3\lambda + \lambda^2 - 8 = 0$$
    $$\lambda^2 - 4\lambda - 5 = 0$$
    $$(\lambda - 5)(\lambda + 1) = 0$$
    The eigenvalues are **$\lambda_1 = 5$** and **$\lambda_2 = -1$**.
2.  **Eigenvector for $\lambda_1 = 5$:**
    $$D - 5I = \begin{pmatrix} -4 & 4 \\ 2 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$$
    Solving $\begin{pmatrix} -4 & 4 \\ 2 & -2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ yields $-x + y = 0$, so $x = y$.
    Let $x = 1$, then $y = 1$. The eigenvector is **$\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$**.
3.  **Eigenvector for $\lambda_2 = -1$:**
    $$D - (-1)I = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$$
    Solving $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ yields $2x + 4y = 0$, so $x = -2y$.
    Let $y = 1$, then $x = -2$. The eigenvector is **$\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$**.
