Salvage Negotiations

A relentless downpour battered the corrugated tin roof of the temporary command pavilion, composing a deafening symphony of metallic clatter that rang out from every direction through the damp air. Inside, the atmosphere was barely breathable — the air thick with the sour stench of cheap, burnt coffee and the inescapable exhalation of estuary mud. Outside, in the purplish dark of the foggy storm, the listing hull of H.M.S. Leviathan was wedged hopelessly between the treacherous shoals of the coastal inlet. A single tungsten bulb swung hard from the ceiling, pendulous and heavy, casting lurching shadows across the faces of two men — two men locked in a hopeless impasse over a salvage operation.

Alistair Vance, a man whose immaculate, bespoke overcoat seemed a deliberate affront to the surrounding squalor, stood rigidly by the fogged window. His posture called to mind the lines of a classical statue — impenetrable, steeped in ancestral authority. Across from him, slouched carelessly on a rusted folding chair, mud-caked boots propped insolently on the notched planks of the table, sat Jacksie — master of the salvage crew, presently engaged in the murky business of extracting a sizeable portion of Vance's fortune from the deep, and getting precisely nowhere.

"Look, mate," said Jacksie, worrying a chewed toothpick between his teeth. "Here's the situation: that boat's stuck good and proper, God help it. The mud down there's like glue. I haven't got a magic wand to just pop it loose. You want it out, we've got to go in hard. Simple as that. My lads are down there, in the dark, brother. Total blackout. It's chaos down there — twisted metal, burst pipes, mud thick as concrete. You can't just walk in and clip a tow cable on straight. You need a bit of finesse, yeah? Craft."

Alistair turned slowly, his face set in an expression of deep, aristocratic exhaustion. "Your primitive assessment notwithstanding, the suggestion of applying destructive measures runs entirely contrary to our objectives. I find it wholly aggravating — this pedestrian jargon with which you characterise what is, in fact, an engineering undertaking of the highest sophistication. The Leviathan is not some common barge to be 'craftily' wrenched from the mud. She is a vessel equipped with experimental quantum arrays whose precision cannot be overstated. Even the slightest degree of lateral deviation could permanently misalign the optical matrices. I insist you set aside this supplementary proposal of yours at once. It is a remarkably novel approach, but entirely unacceptable."

Jacksie wrinkled his nose and flicked the toothpick onto the floorboards. "Set aside? Mate, if we set it aside, we're doing nothing at all. And 'novel'? I'm not reading a sci-fi novel here, pal. I'm trying to save your operation. You're lecturing me about the situation but you can't see the actual current. The river current out there is running at ten knots. And that stray current from your busted generators? Christ, water conducts electricity. It's a death trap."

"Such florid hyperbole does nothing to relieve the underlying contractual obligations," Alistair replied in a glacial tone. "The terms of our agreement were clearly formalised. Characterising a standard maritime salvage operation as a 'death trap' is a dramatic simplification of a hydrodynamic equation that is mathematically solvable. I am fully cognisant of the hydrological and electrical realities. Nevertheless, standing on the shore this morning, I happened to observe a white heron resting alone on the riverbank. It remained entirely unmoved by the noise of that wretched, clanking mechanical crane you have parked so carelessly close by. Speaking of the shore — my superiors at the Bank of England are growing increasingly intolerant of your protracted delays."

"Are they now? Then tell your cabinet men to come down and take a stroll along that bank," Jacksie said, making a sweeping gesture toward the window. "It's pure bog. Their fancy Italian shoes will be swallowed up in two seconds flat. My pumps have blown, the hoses are leaking, and the winch'll blow a gasket with one bad pull. We're running on fuel dregs, gaffer tape, and sheer stubbornness."

Jacksie leaned forward and dropped his boots onto the floor with a heavy thud, rattling the coffee cups. "Alistair, I can't keep playing this game. We need to decide right now. There's an elephant in the room, namely the fact that your ship is literally half rotted through. Get to the point. You want the mainframe computers out of the hold, but you seem to think the heavy lifting equipment is too dear."

Alistair stiffened, adjusting the wire frame of his spectacles. "Resorting to tired clichés does nothing to strengthen our mutual enterprise. I have studied your revised contract's draft proposal in considerable detail, as well as the structural drawings of the storage hold. The financial draw this requires from our operational reserves is tantamount to extortion, particularly given that the vessel, in its current state, presents a surprisingly shallow draft. Furthermore, standing here at this shattered window, I am subjected to a most disagreeable draught."

"Then put a jumper on," Jacksie growled, rolling his eyes up toward the tin roof. "There are thirty men freezing in the rain outside. I won't ask them to drown so you can save a few quid. If you don't authorise the hazard rate, I'll lay everything out in front of the union reps. Then they'll say 'we're off,' wash their hands of it, and leave your precious cargo boxes sitting in the mud."

Alistair closed his eyes and pinched the bridge of his nose — the supreme gesture of a man exercising restraint. "I find it unnecessary to remind you of the catastrophic fiasco we endured in the North Sea two years ago. Corporate management dismissed the union representatives for fear of cutting into profit. The result was an operation riddled with glaring logistical failures. During the extraction phase, heavy iron chains crushed the wooden support pallets because they were far too weak to bear the kinetic load. I cannot permit the repetition of errors of that nature."

Jacksie shook his head with a mirthless expression. "Mate, I don't care about the North Sea. I care about right now. Are you going to sign the work order, or not?"

Alistair's voice dropped — glacial, authoritative, infused with bureaucratic severity. "Listen to me carefully, Jacksie. Should the International Maritime Tribunal determine that the structural integrity of the hull was already irreparably compromised prior to our present intervention, it is imperative that liability fall entirely upon your syndicate, whose evident negligence has been thoroughly and comprehensively documented."

Jacksie stared at him blankly. "That… what are you on about?"

Alistair continued, his tone immovable. "It was strictly stipulated by the lead underwriters that extraction be halted immediately pending the collapse of the second bulkhead — a situation which, had your engineering team given it adequate consideration, would have been entirely avoidable through reinforcement protocols that, for inexplicable reasons, were never carried out."

Jacksie rubbed his temples, unable to suppress a laugh. "Mate, all bark and no bite. But fine. You want to talk liability? The bark is peeling off those wooden pilings over there because your ship sailed straight into them. That wasn't us. And don't get me started on your security detail either — their guard dogs were barking non-stop and kept my lads awake all night."

Alistair exhaled heavily — his voice nearly swallowed by the relentless drumming of the rain. "This stretch of water, this treacherous coastal inlet, has demonstrated considerably more force than our initial acoustic soundings indicated. The ambient noise of the storm completely masks the groan of the metal. What I require is a competent operational strategy — not some reckless, impulsive game."

"You want a competent strategy?" Jacksie rose to his feet, zipping his high-visibility vest up to the chin. "Pay the hazard rate. Give my lads an incentive. In that case, we secure the pontoons, we pump the ballast, and your precious cargo comes up to the surface. Otherwise, it stays on the bottom forever. Your call, boss."

Alistair stared hard through the window at the grey, rain-battered pane of glass, watching the drops fall against the surface. "Despite the board of directors repeatedly urging me to consign the sunken cargo to unrelenting depths, my firm conviction prevailed — recovery must be the priority, on the condition that divers who are properly briefed on the hazardous environment are guaranteed that their substantial compensation will be paid, even in the event of catastrophic operational failure."

Jacksie smiled — a wide flash of white teeth gleaming in the dim, shadowed light of the pavilion. "Now you're speaking my language, mate. In ten minutes the crew'll be geared up and in the water."

"I sincerely doubt I shall ever speak your language, Jacksie," Alistair murmured, turning his back to the window as the salvage master clattered out into the howling storm. "But I hope, for both our sakes, this wretched affair will yield something worthwhile in the end."
