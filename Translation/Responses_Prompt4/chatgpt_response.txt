Negotiation over the Recovery of the Ship

The unceasing downpour hammered the corrugated metal roof of the temporary command pavilion, creating a deafening symphony of metallic drumming that reverberated through the humid air. Inside, the atmosphere was stifling, thick with the acrid smell of burnt cheap coffee and the unmistakable stench of estuarine mud. Outside, wrapped in the bleak gray of the squall, the tilted carcass of the H.M.S. Leviathan lay hopelessly wedged against the treacherous shoals of the coastal inlet. A single tungsten bulb swung violently above their heads, casting erratic, pendulum-like shadows across the faces of the two men trapped in a bitter impasse over the recovery operation.
Alistair Vance, a man whose impeccable bespoke trench coat seemed a deliberate affront to the surrounding filth, stood rigid beside the fogged window. His posture was that of a classical statue, unbending and steeped in ancestral authority. Opposite him, sprawled carelessly on a rusty folding chair, his mud-caked boots brazenly propped on the scarred wooden table, was Jax. Jax was the foreman of the slapdash salvage outfit that was, at that very moment, failing to extract Vance’s multimillion-dollar asset from the murky depths.
"Listen, pal," Jax grumbled, chewing aggressively on a frayed toothpick. "Bottom line? This thing is stuck bad. That mud out there is like glue. It’s not like I’ve got a magic wand to wave and make it pop free. If you want it out, we gotta crack it open in two. Simple. My boys are down there in the dark, man. Pitch black. It’s a complete shitshow out there. We’ve got twisted metal, busted pipes, and mud thick as cement. It’s not like you just stroll up and slap on a tow line. You gotta work it, y’know? Treat it gentle."
Alistair turned slowly, wearing an expression of profound, aristocratic weariness. "Even allowing for your rudimentary assessment, the proposal to employ destructive measures is entirely antithetical to our objectives. I find your colloquial characterization of this exceedingly delicate engineering undertaking profoundly irritating. The Leviathan is not some mere barge to be 'worked' out of the silt. She is a vessel equipped with experimental quantum arrays, the sensitivity of which cannot be overstated. A mere fraction of a degree of lateral movement could permanently misalign the optical matrices. I must insist that this supplementary proposal of yours be discarded immediately. It is an extraordinarily novel approach, and yet it remains wholly untenable."
Jax snorted, flicking the toothpick onto the floorboards. "Discarded? Brother, if we discard it, we do nothing. And 'novel'? I’m not reading a sci-fi novel here, pretty boy. I’m trying to save your ass. You keep lecturing me about the current situation, but you’re not looking at the actual current. The river current out there is pulling at ten knots. And the electric current from your busted generators? It’s straight-up electrifying the water. It’s one total death trap."
"Such vivid hyperbole does very little to mitigate the underlying contractual obligations," Alistair replied coldly. "The parameters of our agreement were explicitly codified. Your insistence on describing a routine maritime recovery as a 'death trap' constitutes a dramatic oversimplification of a mathematically solvable hydrostatic equation. I am fully cognizant of the hydrological and electrical realities. Nevertheless, while I stood upon the bank this morning, I happened to observe a solitary white crane nesting peacefully on the riverbank. It remained entirely undisturbed by the cacophony of that monstrous mechanical crane you have parked so precariously nearby. Speaking of banks, my superiors at the central financial bank in London are becoming unusually impatient with your protracted delays."
"Oh yeah? Well, tell your tie-wearing suits to come down here and take a walk on this bank," Jax shot back, gesturing wildly toward the window. "It’s pure mud. They’d lose those shiny Italian shoes in it in two flat seconds. I’ve got pumps failing, hoses snapping, and a winch one bad pull away from blowing up catastrophically. Right now we’re running on fumes, duct tape, and spite."
Jax leaned forward, dropping his boots to the floor with a heavy thud that made the coffee cups tremble. "Listen, Alistair, we can’t keep dragging this out. We gotta bite the bullet here and now. There’s a giant elephant in the room, and it’s the fact that your boat is literally breaking in half. Let’s cut the crap. You want the servers out of the hull, but you’re too cheap to sign off on the heavy-lift gear."
Alistair stiffened, adjusting his metal-rimmed glasses. "Your reliance on colloquial commonplaces contributes nothing whatsoever to the advancement of our shared enterprise. I have examined with the utmost care the preliminary draft of your revised contract, together with the design draft for the containment container. The financial outlay this would require from our operational reserves is, to put it mildly, extortionate, particularly given the vessel’s surprisingly shallow draft in its current predicament. Furthermore, standing beside this broken pane of glass, I detect a rather terrible draft."
"Then put on a sweater," Jax groaned, rolling his eyes up at the tin roof. "I’ve got thirty men out there freezing their asses off. I’m not asking them to risk drowning just because you want to save a couple bucks. If you don’t authorize hazard pay, I’m gonna sing like a bird to the union reps. And then they’ll walk. They’ll just throw in the towel, man, and leave your glowing boxes in the mud."
Alistair closed his eyes, pinching the bridge of his nose in a supreme display of self-control. "I need not remind you of the catastrophic débâcle we suffered in the North Sea two years ago. The corporate executives dismissed the union representatives because they were terrified of shrinking profit margins. Consequently, the operation proceeded with glaring logistical deficiencies. During the extraction phase, the heavy iron chains crushed the wooden support pallets because they were too fragile to bear the kinetic load. I shall not tolerate a repetition of such staggering ineptitude."
Jax shook his head, looking utterly exasperated. "Man, I don’t give a damn about the North Sea. I care about right now. Are you signing the work order or not?"
Alistair’s voice dropped into a glacial, authoritative register, weighted with bureaucratic finality. "Listen to me with the utmost attention, Jax. Should the international maritime tribunal determine that the structural integrity of the hull had been irredeemably compromised prior to our intervention today, it is imperative that liability be transferred in its entirety to your consortium, whose manifest negligence has been carefully and meticulously documented."
Jax stared at him blankly. "What the hell are you talking about?"
Alistair continued, in an inflexible tone. "It was categorically mandated by the principal underwriters that the extraction be halted immediately in order to avert the failure of the secondary bulkhead, an eventuality which, had it been adequately foreseen by your team of engineers, would have been entirely prevented by the reinforcement protocols that were, inexplicably, discarded."
Jax rubbed his temples, laughing without amusement. "Man, your bark’s a lot worse than your bite. But fine. You want to talk liability? The bark’s peeling off those wooden piles out there because your ship slammed into them. We didn’t do that. And don’t even get me started on the security crew you dragged along with you; the nonstop barking from their guard dogs kept my team awake the whole goddamn night."
Alistair sighed heavily, a sound barely audible above the incessant hammering of the rain. "This body of water, this treacherous coastal inlet, has proved considerably more formidable than our initial acoustic surveys indicated. The ambient sound of the storm is completely masking the groans of the steel. I am merely requesting a sound operational strategy, not a reckless and impulsive gamble."
"You want a sound strategy?" Jax stood up, yanking the zipper of his high-visibility jacket aggressively up to his chin. "Pay the hazard rate. Give my boys something worth bleeding for. Do that, and we hook up the pontoons, blow the ballast, and bring your precious cargo back up to the surface. Don’t do it, and she stays on the bottom forever. Your call, boss."
Alistair stared deeply into the boiling gray abyss beyond the window, watching the rain strike the glass. "Although it has been repeatedly suggested by the board of directors that the submerged cargo be abandoned to the implacable depths, it is my firm conviction that recovery must remain the priority, provided that the divers, being duly apprised of the context of extreme peril, are assured that their exorbitant compensation shall be guaranteed in the event that the operation is catastrophically compromised."
Jax smiled, a flash of white teeth that pierced the dim, shadowy light of the pavilion. "Now you’re speaking my language, pretty boy. I’ll get the crew ready and send ’em into the water in ten."
"I sincerely doubt I shall ever speak your language, Jax," Alistair murmured, turning his back to the window as the salvage foreman stepped out of the tent into the howling storm. "But let us hope, for both our sakes, that this wretched enterprise finally bears fruit."
