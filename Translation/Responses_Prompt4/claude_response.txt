Negotiation for the Recovery of the Wreck

The relentless downpour hammered the corrugated metal roof of the makeshift command pavilion, creating a deafening symphony of metallic rolls that resonated through the damp air. Inside, the atmosphere was suffocating, thick with the acrid smell of cheap, burnt coffee and the unmistakable stench of estuarine mud. Outside, shrouded in the grim grey of the storm, the listing hull of H.M.S. Leviathan lay hopelessly grounded against the treacherous shallows of the coastal inlet. A single tungsten bulb swung violently overhead, casting irregular shadows, like the motion of a pendulum, across the faces of two men locked in a bitter stalemate over the question of the salvage operation.

Alistair Vance, a man whose immaculate, bespoke mackintosh seemed a deliberate affront to the squalid misery surrounding him, stood rigid beside the fogged window. His posture was that of a classical statue, immovable and steeped in ancestral authority. Opposite him, sprawled negligently on a rusted folding chair with his mud-caked boots propped shamelessly on the scarred wooden table, sat Jax. Jax was the foreman of the ramshackle salvage crew that was failing in its attempt to extract Vance's multi-million-pound asset from the silty seabed.

"Look, mate," Jax muttered, aggressively chewing a frayed toothpick. "Here's the deal: the thing is properly stuck. The mud out there is like glue. I haven't got a magic wand to wave and pull her out. If you want her salvaged, we have to cut into her. That's it, full stop. My guys are down there in pitch black, you get me? Total darkness. It's an absolute shambles out there. We've got twisted metal, busted pipes, and mud thick as concrete. You can't just waltz in and clip a tow cable to it. You've got to work it carefully, yeah? Delicately."

Alistair turned slowly, wearing an expression of profound, aristocratic fatigue. "Notwithstanding your rudimentary assessment, the proposal to resort to destructive measures is entirely at odds with our objectives. I find your colloquial characterisation of this engineering undertaking of extreme delicacy to be exceedingly irritating. The Leviathan is not some common barge to be 'worked carefully' out of the mud. She is a vessel equipped with experimental quantum arrays, the sensitivity of which cannot be overstated. A minimal fraction of a degree of lateral displacement could permanently misalign the optical matrices. I must insist that this supplementary scheme of yours be set aside immediately. It is a surprisingly original approach, and yet it remains wholly untenable."

Jax snorted, tossing the toothpick onto the floor. "Set it aside? Brother, if we set it aside, we do nothing. And 'original'? I'm not reading a science fiction novel, mate. I'm trying to get you out of a hole. You keep lecturing me about the current situation, but you're not looking at the actual current. The river current out there is running at ten knots. And the electrical current from your busted generators? It's literally electrifying the water. It's a proper death trap."

"Such vivid hyperbole contributes nothing whatsoever to mitigating the underlying contractual obligations," replied Alistair, in a glacial tone. "The parameters of our agreement were explicitly codified. Your insistence on characterising a standard maritime salvage as a 'death trap' is a dramatic oversimplification of a mathematically solvable hydrostatic equation. I am fully aware of the hydrological and electrical realities. Nevertheless, while I was standing at the water's edge this morning, I had occasion to observe a solitary white heron nesting quietly on the riverbank. It remained entirely undisturbed by the cacophony of that dreadful mechanical crane you have parked so precariously nearby. On the subject of banks, my superiors at the central bank in London have become unusually impatient regarding your prolonged delays."

"Oh, have they? Then tell your bigwigs to come down here and take a stroll along this bank," Jax shot back, gesticulating frantically toward the window. "It's pure bog. They'd lose their fancy Italian shoes in two seconds flat. I've got pumps giving out, pipes bursting, and a winch one step away from blowing to pieces. We're running on fumes, gaffer tape, and sheer bloody-mindedness."

Jax leaned forward, dropping his boots to the floor with a heavy thud that rattled the coffee cups. "Look, Alistair, we can't keep kicking the can down the road forever. We need to bite the bullet here and now. There's a massive elephant in the room, and it's the fact that your boat is literally breaking in two. Let's just cut to the chase. You want the servers out of the hull, but you're too tight-fisted to authorise the heavy-lift machinery."

Alistair stiffened, adjusting his metal-framed spectacles. "Your recourse to colloquial clichés does not advance our joint venture in any way. I have carefully examined the preliminary draft of your revised contract, together with the architectural design of the containment unit. The financial levy that would result from our operational reserves is nothing short of extortionate, especially given the surprisingly shallow draught of the vessel in its current predicament. What is more, standing next to this shattered panel, I find myself subjected to a rather irritating draught."

"Then put a jumper on," groaned Jax, rolling his eyes toward the metal roof. "I've got thirty men out there freezing their backsides off. I'm not going to ask them to risk drowning just because you want to pinch pennies. If you don't authorise the hazard allowance, I'm going to spill the beans to the union reps. And then they'll walk. They'll throw in the towel, mate, and leave your fancy glowing boxes in the mud."

Alistair closed his eyes, pinching the bridge of his nose in a supreme act of self-control. "I hardly need to remind you of the catastrophic disaster we were forced to contend with in the North Sea two years ago. The company executives dismissed the union representatives because they were terrified by the declining profit margins. Consequently, the operation proceeded with glaring logistical deficiencies. During the extraction phase, the heavy iron chains crushed the wooden support pallets because they were too fragile to bear the kinetic load. I will not tolerate the repetition of such colossal incompetence."

Jax shook his head, his expression one of utter exasperation. "Mate, I couldn't give a toss about the North Sea. I care about right now. Are you signing the work order or not?"

Alistair's voice dropped to a cold, authoritative register, laden with bureaucratic finality. "Listen to me very carefully, Jax. In the event that the international maritime tribunal should determine that the structural integrity of the hull had been irreparably compromised prior to our intervention today, it is imperative that liability fall entirely upon your consortium, whose manifest negligence has been thoroughly and meticulously documented."

Jax stared at him blankly. "I'm sorry, what did you just say?"

Alistair continued, his tone unyielding. "It had been expressly stipulated by the lead insurers that extraction be immediately suspended so as to prevent the failure of the second bulkhead, an eventuality which, had it been adequately anticipated by your team of engineers, would have been entirely averted by the reinforcement protocols that were, inexplicably, discarded."

Jax rubbed his temples, laughing without humour. "Mate, your bark is a lot worse than your bite. But fine. You want to talk about liability? The bark on those wooden piles out there is peeling off because your ship rammed into them. That wasn't us. And don't even get me started on the security detail you brought along — their guard dogs with all that constant barking kept my whole crew up all night."

Alistair exhaled deeply, the sound barely audible above the relentless drumming of the rain. "This body of water, this treacherous coastal inlet, has proven far more formidable than our initial acoustic surveys indicated. The ambient sound of the storm is completely masking the creaking of the steel. I simply need a sound operational strategy, not a reckless and impulsive gamble."

"You want a sound strategy?" Jax rose to his feet, zipping his high-visibility jacket up to his chin with a sharp tug. "Pay the hazard rate. Give my guys a personal stake in it. If you do that, we anchor the pontoons, vent the ballast, and bring your precious cargo back up to the surface. If you don't, it stays on the bottom forever. Your call, boss."

Alistair stared deeply into the churning grey abyss beyond the window, watching the rain strike the glass. "Although it has been repeatedly suggested by the board of directors that we abandon the submerged cargo to the inexorable depths, I remain firmly convinced that recovery must be prioritised, on the condition that the divers, who have been duly briefed on the hazardous environment, are guaranteed that their considerable remuneration will be secured in the eventuality that the operation is catastrophically compromised."

Jax smiled, a flash of white teeth cutting through the dim, shadowed light of the pavilion. "Now you're talking sense, mate. I'll get the crew ready and have them in the water in ten minutes."

"I sincerely doubt I shall ever speak your language, Jax," murmured Alistair, turning his back to the window as the salvage foreman stepped out of the tent into the howling storm. "But let us hope, for the sake of us both, that this wretched enterprise finally bears fruit."
