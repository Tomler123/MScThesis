Salvage Negotiations

The incessant downpour battered the corrugated iron roof of the temporary command pavilion, creating a deafening symphony of metallic drumming that reverberated in the humid air. Inside, the atmosphere was stifling, permeated with the acrid stench of cheap, burnt coffee and the unmistakable reek of estuarine mud. Outside, shrouded in the gray gloom of the squall, the listing hull of the *Leviathan* was hopelessly marooned on the treacherous shoals of the coastal strait. A lone tungsten bulb swung wildly overhead, casting restless, pendulum-like shadows across the faces of two men who had reached a dead end in their argument over the salvage operation.

Alistair Vance, a man whose immaculate, bespoke trench coat seemed an intentional insult to the surrounding ruin, stood frozen by the fogged-up window. His posture resembled an antique statue, unyielding and full of ancestral grandeur. Opposite him, casually sprawled in a rusty folding chair with his mud-caked boots brazenly propped up on the scratched wooden table, sat Jax. Jax was the foreman of the motley salvage crew currently failing to extract Vance’s multi-million-dollar asset from the murky depths.

"Look, man," muttered Jax, aggressively chewing a frayed toothpick. "Bottom line? This thing is stuck tight. The mud out there is like glue. I ain't got a magic wand to wave and just yank it out. You want it out — we gotta cut the hull open. Pure and simple. My guys are down there in the dark, dude. Can't see a damn thing. It's a total shitshow. Twisted metal, busted pipes, and mud thick as concrete. You can't just waltz in and slap a towline on it. You gotta massage it, you feel me? Finesse it."

Alistair turned slowly, his face expressing a profound, aristocratic weariness. "Notwithstanding your primitive assessment, the proposition to employ destructive measures is entirely unacceptable for our purposes. Your colloquial description of this highly delicate engineering endeavor grates on my ears. The *Leviathan* is not some barge that can be 'massaged' out of the silt. It is a vessel equipped with experimental quantum arrays, the sensitivity of which cannot be overstated. A horizontal displacement of even a fraction of a degree could result in an irreversible misalignment of the optical matrices. I must insist that we immediately table this supplementary proposal of yours. It is a highly novel approach, yet it is absolutely unsuitable."

Jax snorted, flicking his toothpick onto the floorboards. "Table it? Bro, if we table it, we ain't getting nowhere. And 'novel'? I ain't writing sci-fi novels here, pal. I'm trying to save your ass. You keep lecturing me about the current situation, but you ain't looking at the actual current. The river current out there is ripping at ten knots. And the electrical current from your busted generators? It's zapping right into the water. That's certain death."

"Such colorful hyperbole does nothing to mitigate your underlying contractual obligations," Alistair replied in an icy tone. "The parameters of our agreement were clearly stipulated. Your insistence on characterizing a standard maritime salvage operation as 'certain death' is a dramatic oversimplification of a mathematically solvable hydrostatic equation. I am perfectly cognizant of the hydrological and electrical realities. Nevertheless, standing at the water's edge this morning, I happened to notice a solitary white crane nesting peacefully on the riverbank. It was entirely undisturbed by the cacophony of that monstrous construction crane you so recklessly parked nearby. And, while we are on the subject of rivers and banks, allow me to note: my superiors at the central financial bank in London are growing exceptionally impatient with your protracted delays."

"Yeah? Well tell your suits to come down here and take a stroll along this very bank," Jax snapped, gesturing angrily toward the window. "It's pure muck out there. They'll lose their shiny Italian loafers in two seconds flat. I got pumps failing, hoses bursting, and the winch is one bad yank away from a catastrophic failure. We're running on fumes, duct tape, and pure spite right now."

Jax leaned forward, dropping his boots to the floor with a heavy thud that made the coffee mugs rattle. "Listen, Alistair, we can't keep kicking the can down the road. We need to bite the bullet and make a call right here, right now. There's a massive elephant in the room we're ignoring, and that's the fact that your boat is literally snapping in half. Let's cut to the chase. You want to rip the servers out of the hull, but you're too cheap to pony up for the heavy lifting gear."

Alistair frowned, adjusting his wire-rimmed glasses. "Your use of colloquial clichés does nothing to advance our joint enterprise. I have subjected the preliminary draft of your revised contract to rigorous analysis, along with the architectural draft of the containment tank. The financial transfer it would require from our operating reserves is nothing short of extortion, particularly given the vessel's surprisingly shallow draft in its current predicament. Furthermore, standing near this broken pane, I am feeling a rather severe cold draft."

"Then put on a sweater," Jax groaned, rolling his eyes at the tin roof. "I got thirty guys out there freezing their asses off. I ain't about to ask 'em to risk drowning just so you can save a couple bucks. If you don't sign off on hazard pay, I'm spilling my guts to the union reps. And then they walk. They'll just wash their hands of it, man, and leave your glowing boxes right in the mud."

Alistair closed his eyes, pinching the bridge of his nose in a display of supreme self-restraint. "I have no need to remind you of the catastrophic debacle we suffered in the North Sea two years ago. The corporate board brushed off the union board because they were terrified of shrinking profit margins. Consequently, the operation proceeded with egregious logistical oversights. During the extraction phase, heavy iron chains crushed the wooden support boards, as they were entirely too brittle to withstand the kinetic load. I will not countenance a repetition of such glaring incompetence."

Jax shook his head, looking completely at his wits' end. "Dude, I don't give a rat's ass about the North Sea. I care about what's happening right now. Are you signing the work order or not?"

Alistair's voice dropped to an icy, authoritative register, laden with bureaucratic finality. "Listen to me very carefully, Jax. Should the international maritime tribunal determine that the hull's structural integrity was irreparably compromised prior to our intervention today, it will be absolutely imperative to shift all liability entirely onto your syndicate, whose gross negligence has been thoroughly and meticulously documented."

Jax stared at him blankly. "Say what?"

Alistair continued in an unyielding tone. "The principal underwriters have strictly mandated an immediate suspension of the extraction to avoid fracturing the secondary bulkhead—a contingency which, had it been properly anticipated by your engineering team, could have been entirely averted via the reinforcement protocols you inexplicably abandoned."

Jax rubbed his temples, laughing humorlessly. "Man, your bark is worse than your bite. But fine. You wanna talk liability? The bark on those wooden pilings outside is peeling off because your ship slammed into them at full speed. We didn't do that. And don't even get me started on that security detail you brought in; the incessant bark of their guard dogs kept my crew awake all damn night."

Alistair sighed heavily, the sound barely audible over the relentless drumming of the rain. "This body of water, this treacherous coastal sound, has proven far more formidable than our initial acoustic soundings indicated. The ambient sound of the tempest completely masks the grinding of the steel. All I require of you is a sound operational strategy, not some reckless, impulsive gambit."

"You want a sound strategy?" Jax stood up, aggressively zipping his hi-vis jacket all the way to his chin. "Pay the hazard premium. Give my guys some skin in the game. Do that, and we'll rig the pontoons, blow the ballast, and bring your precious cargo to the surface. Don't, and she stays on the bottom forever. Your call, boss."

Alistair gazed deeply into the gray, churning abyss beyond the window, watching the rain batter the glass. "Although the board of directors has repeatedly proposed abandoning the sunken cargo to the unforgiving depths, I remain firmly convinced that extraction must be prioritized, provided that the divers, having been sufficiently apprised of the hazardous environment, receive assurances that their exorbitant compensation will be guaranteed in the event the operation culminates in disaster."

Jax grinned, the flash of his white teeth cutting through the dim, murky light of the pavilion. "Now you're speaking my language, pal. I'll prep the crew, and they'll be in the water in ten minutes."

"I sincerely doubt I shall ever speak your language, Jax," Alistair murmured, turning away from the window as the salvage foreman exited the tent into the howling gale. "But let us hope, for both our sakes, that this wretched enterprise finally bears fruit."
