Negotiation on the Recovery of the Ship

The incessant downpour hammered the corrugated tin roof of the temporary command pavilion, creating a deafening symphony of metallic pattering that echoed in the humid air. Inside, the atmosphere was stifling, thick with the acrid smell of cheap, burnt coffee and the unmistakable stench of estuarine mud. Outside, wrapped in the gloomy gray of the storm, the tilted carcass of the H.M.S. Leviathan lay hopelessly wedged against the dangerous sandbanks of the coastal inlet. A single tungsten bulb swung violently above their heads, casting erratic, pendulum-like shadows across the faces of the two men locked in a bitter impasse over the recovery operation.

Alistair Vance, a man whose impeccable bespoke trench coat seemed a deliberate affront to the surrounding squalor, stood rigidly beside the fogged window. His posture was that of a classical statue, unyielding and steeped in ancestral authority. Facing him, slouched negligently on a rusty folding chair with his mud-caked boots brazenly propped on the scarred wooden table, was Jax. Jax was the foreman of the ragtag recovery crew that was currently failing to extract Vance’s multimillion-dollar asset from the murky depths.

«Look, buddy,» muttered Jax, aggressively chewing a frayed toothpick. «Bottom line? The stuff is stuck real bad. The mud out there is like glue. There’s no magic wand I can wave to make it pop out. If you want it out, we gotta open it up. Simple as. My boys are down there in the dark, buddy. Total blackout conditions. It’s a complete mess out there. We’ve got twisted metal, broken pipes, and mud thick as cement. You don’t just waltz in and hook up a tow cable. You gotta work it, you know? Use finesse.»

Alistair turned slowly, his expression one of profound, aristocratic weariness. «Apart from your rudimentary assessment, the proposal to employ destructive measures is entirely anathema to our objectives. I find your colloquial characterization of this highly delicate engineering endeavor extremely irritating. The Leviathan is not a simple barge to be ‘massaged’ out of the silt. It is a vessel equipped with experimental quantum arrays whose sensitivity cannot be overstated. A mere fraction of a degree in lateral movement could permanently misalign the optical matrices. I must insist that we immediately set aside this supplementary proposal of yours. It is an extremely innovative approach, yet it remains wholly unsustainable.»

Jax snorted, tossing the toothpick onto the floorboards. «Set it aside? Bro, if we set it aside, we do nothing. And ‘innovative’? I’m not reading a sci-fi novel here, buddy. I’m trying to save your ass. You keep lecturing me about the current situation, but you’re not looking at the actual current. The river current out there is ripping at ten knots. And the electrical current from your busted generators? It’s actively frying the water. It’s a total death trap.»

«Such vivid hyperbole does little to mitigate the underlying contractual obligations,» replied Alistair, his tone glacial. «The parameters of our agreement were explicitly codified. Your insistence on characterizing standard maritime salvage as a ‘death trap’ is a dramatic and excessive oversimplification of a mathematically solvable hydrostatic equation. I am acutely aware of the hydrological and electrical realities. However, while I was standing at the water’s edge this morning, I had the opportunity to observe a solitary white crane nesting peacefully on the riverbank. It remained utterly undisturbed by the racket of that monstrous mechanical crane you parked so precariously nearby. Speaking of the bank, my superiors at the central financial bank in London are becoming unusually impatient with your prolonged delays.»

«Yeah? Well, tell your fancy boys to come down here and take a walk on this bank,» retorted Jax, gesturing wildly toward the window. «It’s pure mud. They’d lose their shiny Italian shoes in two seconds flat. I’ve got pumps failing, hoses snapping, and a winch that’s one bad pull away from a catastrophic explosion. We’re running on steam, duct tape, and spite right now.»

Jax leaned forward, dropping his boots to the floor with a heavy thud that made the coffee cups rattle. «Look, Alistair, we can’t keep putting off the problem. We gotta grit our teeth right here and now. There’s a huge elephant in the room, and it’s the fact that your boat is literally breaking in two. Let’s cut to the chase. You want the servers out of the hull, but you’re too cheap to authorize the heavy lifting equipment.»

Alistair stiffened, adjusting his wire-rimmed glasses. «Your deployment of colloquial clichés does nothing to advance our common enterprise. I have carefully examined the preliminary draft of your revised contract, along with the architectural draft of the container. The financial draft that would be required from our operating reserves is nothing short of extortionate, especially given the surprisingly shallow immersion of the vessel in its current situation. Moreover, standing adjacent to this broken glass, I am catching a terrible cold draft.»

«Then put on a sweater,» groaned Jax, rolling his eyes at the tin roof. «I’ve got thirty guys out there freezing their asses off. I’m not gonna ask them to risk drowning just to save you a few bucks. If you don’t authorize the hazard pay, I’ll spill everything to the union reps. Then they’ll walk. They’ll throw in the towel, buddy, and leave your glowing boxes in the mud.»

Alistair closed his eyes, pinching the bridge of his nose in a supreme display of self-control. «I do not need to remind you of the catastrophic disaster we suffered in the North Sea two years ago. The corporate executives rejected the union representatives because they were terrified of shrinking profit margins. As a result, the operation proceeded with evident logistical shortcomings. During the extraction phase, the heavy iron chains crushed the wooden support pallets because they were too fragile to withstand the kinetic load. I will not tolerate a repetition of such disconcerting incompetence.»

Jax shook his head, looking utterly exasperated. «Buddy, I don’t care about the North Sea. I care about here and now. You signing the work order or not?»

Alistair’s voice dropped to a glacial, authoritarian register, laden with bureaucratic finality. «Listen to me very carefully, Jax. Should it be determined by the international maritime court that the structural integrity of the hull had been irreparably compromised prior to our intervention today, it is imperative that liability be transferred entirely to your union, whose glaring negligence has been thoroughly and meticulously documented.»

Jax stared blankly. «What did you just say?»

Alistair continued, his tone inflexible. «It has been strictly ordered by the lead insurers that the extraction be suspended immediately so that the secondary bulkhead does not fail—a contingency that, had it been properly anticipated by your team of engineers, would have been entirely avoided by the reinforcement protocols that were, inexplicably, discarded.»

Jax massaged his temples, laughing without humor. «Buddy, you bark more than you bite. But fine. You wanna talk about liability? The bark on those wooden poles out there is peeling because your ship crashed into them. It wasn’t us. And don’t even get me started on the security team you brought; the nonstop barking of their guard dogs kept my crew awake all night.»

Alistair sighed heavily, the sound barely audible above the incessant pattering of the rain. «This body of water, this treacherous coastal inlet, has proven far more formidable than indicated by our initial acoustic surveys. The ambient sound of the storm is completely masking the groans of the steel. I am asking only for a solid operational strategy, not a reckless and impulsive gamble.»

«You want a solid strategy?» Jax stood up, aggressively zipping his high-visibility jacket up to his chin. «Pay the hazard rate. Give my guys a stake. Do that, and we secure the pontoons, empty the ballast, and float your precious cargo to the surface. You don’t, and it stays on the bottom forever. Your call, boss.»

Alistair stared deeply into the gray, turbulent abyss outside the window, watching the rain strike the glass. «Although it has been repeatedly suggested by the board of directors that the submerged cargo be abandoned to the implacable depths, it is my firm conviction that the recovery must be prioritized, provided that the divers, who have been sufficiently informed of the hazardous environment, are assured that their exorbitant compensation will be guaranteed in the event that the operation is catastrophically compromised.»

Jax smiled, a flash of white teeth piercing the dim, shadowy light of the pavilion. «Now you’re speaking my language, buddy. I’ll get the team ready and put them in the water in ten minutes.»

«I sincerely doubt I will ever speak your language, Jax,» murmured Alistair, turning his back to the window as the recovery foreman exited the tent into the howling storm. «But let us hope, for both our sakes, that this miserable enterprise finally bears fruit.»
