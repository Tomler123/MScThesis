Negotiations Over Raising a Sunken Vessel

The endless downpour lashed the corrugated tin roof of the temporary command pavilion, creating a deafening symphony of metallic shot that reverberated through the damp air. Inside, the atmosphere was suffocating, thick with the acrid smell of cheap burnt coffee and the unmistakable stench of estuarine mud. Outside, concealed by the gray murk of the squall, the listing hulk of H.M.S. “Leviathan” lay hopelessly stuck on the treacherous shallows of the coastal strait. A lone tungsten bulb swung furiously overhead, casting erratic, pendulum-like shadows across the faces of the two men who had reached a bitter impasse over the salvage operation.
Alistair Vance, a man whose immaculate bespoke trench coat seemed a deliberate affront to the filth around him, stood at the fogged window, ramrod straight. There was something of a classical statue in his bearing — unyielding, steeped in ancestral authority. Opposite him, sprawled carelessly on a rusty folding chair and unceremoniously planting his mud-caked boots on the scratched wooden table, sat Jax. Jax was the foreman of the very ragtag salvage outfit that was, at that moment, unsuccessfully trying to haul Vance’s multimillion-dollar asset out of the murky depths.

"Listen, man," — Jax muttered, chewing a frayed toothpick with vicious intensity. "Bottom line? This thing’s stuck hard. That silt down there is like glue. I don’t have a magic wand to just yank her loose, bang, like that. You want her out, we’re gonna have to cut into the hull. Simple as that. My guys are down there in pitch black, man. Zero visibility. Total clusterfuck. Twisted metal, burst pipes, and muck thick as concrete. You can’t just saunter in and hook on a tow cable. You gotta work with her, get me? Delicate. Smart."

Alistair turned slowly, and profound aristocratic weariness showed on his face. "For all the worthlessness of your assessment, the proposal to resort to destructive measures runs entirely counter to our aims. I must confess, your colloquial description of this exceedingly delicate engineering enterprise is fraying my nerves in the extreme. The “Leviathan” is not some barge that can be ‘massaged’ out of the silt. This vessel is fitted with experimental quantum arrays, the sensitivity of which cannot be overstated. A displacement of even a fraction of a degree along the lateral axis could permanently throw the optical matrices out of alignment. I am compelled to insist that we remove your supplemental proposal from consideration at once. The approach is, to be sure, highly innovative, but wholly unacceptable."

Jax snorted and flicked the toothpick onto the floorboards. "Remove it from consideration? Bro, if we remove it from consideration, we ain’t doing anything at all. And what the hell do you mean, ‘innovative’? I’m not reading a science-fiction novel here, pal. I’m trying to save your hide. You keep lecturing me about the current situation, but you’re not even looking at the actual current. The river’s ripping through there at damn near ten knots. And the juice from your busted generators? It’s live in the water right now. That’s not an asset down there, it’s a death trap."

"Such picturesque hyperbole does nothing whatsoever to mitigate the contractual obligations at issue," — Alistair replied in an icy tone. "The parameters of our agreement were set down with the utmost unambiguity. Your insistence on portraying a standard maritime salvage operation as a 'death trap' constitutes a dramatic oversimplification of a hydrostatic equation that is entirely resolvable by mathematical means. I am perfectly well apprised of the hydrological and electrical realities. However, this morning, while standing at the very edge of the water, I happened to notice a solitary white heron nesting peacefully on the riverbank. It appeared utterly untroubled by the cacophony of that monstrous mechanical crane which you have parked so unreliably nearby. Speaking of banks: my superiors at the central financial bank in London are displaying increasingly unusual impatience at your protracted delays."

"Oh yeah? Then tell your suits to come down here themselves and take a stroll along that bank," — Jax snapped, jerking a wild hand toward the window. "It’s pure sludge out there. They’ll bury their shiny Italian shoes in two seconds. My pumps are dying, my hoses are blowing, and the winch is one hard pull away from a catastrophic snap. Right now we’re being held together by a prayer, reinforced duct tape, and pure spite."

Jax leaned forward and slammed his boots down onto the floor with a heavy crash, making the coffee mugs rattle. "Listen, Alistair, we can’t keep kicking this can down the road forever. We’ve gotta grit our teeth and do what needs doing, right here, right now. We got a huge elephant in the room, and it’s that your boat is literally breaking in half. Let’s cut to the chase. You want the servers out of the hull, but you’re too damn cheap to authorize the heavy lift gear."

Alistair stiffened and adjusted his glasses in their thin metal frames. "Your accumulation of conversational clichés does nothing whatsoever to advance our mutual enterprise. I have examined with the utmost care the preliminary draft of your revised estimate, together with the architectural design of the retaining vessel. The financial withdrawals from our operational reserves that this would require can scarcely be called anything but extortionate, particularly in light of the vessel’s astonishingly shallow draft in its present distressed condition. Moreover, while standing beside this shattered frame, I am being subjected to a thoroughly disagreeable draft."

"Then put on a sweater," — Jax groaned, rolling his eyes up at the tin roof. "I got thirty guys out there freezing their asses off. I’m not gonna ask them to risk drowning just so you can save a couple bucks. If you don’t approve hazard pay, I’ll take the whole thing to the union reps. And then they walk. They’ll just throw up their hands, man, and leave your glowing boxes in the mud."

Alistair closed his eyes and, with the greatest composure, pinched the bridge of his nose. "There is no need to remind you of the catastrophic fiasco we endured in the North Sea two years earlier. Corporate management sidelined the union representatives because they were appalled by the shrinking profit margins. As a consequence, the operation proceeded amid flagrant logistical deficiencies. During the extraction phase, heavy iron chains crushed the wooden support pallets, for they were too fragile to bear the kinetic load. I do not intend to permit a repetition of such monstrous incompetence."

Jax shook his head, his whole manner expressing utter exhaustion. "Man, I don’t give a damn about the North Sea. I care about what’s happening right now. You signing the order or not?"

Alistair’s voice dropped into an icy, commanding register, weighted with bureaucratic finality. "Listen to me with the utmost care, Jax. If the international maritime tribunal should determine that the structural integrity of the hull had already been irretrievably compromised prior to our intervention today, then it is of critical importance that all liability be transferred in full to your syndicate, whose glaring negligence has been exhaustively and meticulously documented."

Jax stared at him blankly. "What the hell did you just say?"

Alistair continued in the same unyielding tone. "The primary insurers issued strict instructions that the extraction be halted immediately so as to avoid failure of the secondary bulkhead — a scenario which, had it been properly anticipated by your engineering team, would have been entirely prevented by means of reinforcement protocols that were, for inexplicable reasons, declined."

Jax rubbed his temples and gave a joyless laugh. "Man, you’ve got a lot more bark than bite. But fine. You want to talk liability? The bark’s peeling off those wooden pilings out there because your ship slammed into them. We didn’t do that. And don’t even start on the security goons you brought in: their guard dogs barked so goddamn much my crew didn’t get a wink of sleep all night."

Alistair sighed heavily; the sound could barely be heard over the relentless drumfire of the rain. "This body of water, this treacherous coastal strait, has proven far more formidable than our initial acoustic soundings indicated. The storm’s sonic backdrop entirely drowns out the groaning of the steel. What I require is merely a sound operational strategy, not a reckless, impulsive adventure."

"You want a sound strategy?" — Jax stood and yanked the zipper of his high-visibility jacket all the way up to his chin. "Pay the hazard rate. Give my boys some skin in the game. You do that, we’ll strap on the pontoons, blow the ballast, and bring your precious cargo up to the surface. You don’t, and she stays on the bottom forever. Your call, boss."

Alistair stared intently into the gray, churning abyss beyond the window, watching the rain strike the glass. "Although the board of directors has repeatedly proposed consigning the flooded cargo to the unrelenting depths, I remain firmly convinced that recovery must be given priority, provided that the divers, who were adequately apprised of the deadly environment, are guaranteed their exorbitant compensation in the event that the operation is catastrophically compromised."

Jax grinned, and a flash of white teeth cut through the pavilion’s dull, gloomy light. "Now that’s more like it, pal. I’ll get the crew set up, and in ten minutes they’ll be in the water."

"I sincerely doubt that I shall ever speak your language, Jax," — Alistair murmured, turning away from the window as the salvage foreman stepped out of the tent into the howling storm. "But let us hope, for both our sakes, that this wretched enterprise at last bears fruit."
