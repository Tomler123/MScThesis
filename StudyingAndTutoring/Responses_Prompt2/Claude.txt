## Part (a) — Finding the eigenvalues

We begin by forming the matrix $B - \lambda I$:

$$B - \lambda I = \begin{pmatrix} 4 - \lambda & 1 \\ 2 & 3 - \lambda \end{pmatrix}$$

The characteristic equation is $\det(B - \lambda I) = 0$. We expand the $2 \times 2$ determinant:

$$\det(B - \lambda I) = (4 - \lambda)(3 - \lambda) - (1)(2) = 0$$

Expanding the product:

$$(4 - \lambda)(3 - \lambda) = 12 - 4\lambda - 3\lambda + \lambda^2 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 12$$

Subtracting the off-diagonal product:

$$\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 12 - 2 = 0 \implies \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = 0$$

Factoring:

$$(\lambda - 5)(\lambda - 2) = 0$$

$$\boxed{\lambda_1 = 5, \quad \lambda_2 = 2}$$

---

## Part (b) — Finding the eigenvectors

**Eigenvector for $\lambda_1 = 5$:**

We form $B - 5I$ and row-reduce:

$$B - 5I = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 2 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Both rows carry the same information ($R_2 = -2 R_1$). The single independent equation is:

$$-v_1 + v_2 = 0 \implies v_2 = v_1$$

Setting $v_1 = 1$, the eigenvector is:

$$\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Eigenvector for $\lambda_2 = 2$:**

$$B - 2I = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Again both rows are identical. The single independent equation is:

$$2v_1 + v_2 = 0 \implies v_2 = -2v_1$$

Setting $v_1 = 1$, the eigenvector is:

$$\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix}$$

---

## Part (c) — Verification for $\lambda_1 = 5$

We directly compute $B\mathbf{v}_1$:

$$B\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4(1) + 1(1) \\ 2(1) + 3(1) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}$$

Now compute $\lambda_1 \mathbf{v}_1$:

$$5 \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}$$

Since $B\mathbf{v}_1 = \lambda_1 \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 5 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix}$, the result is confirmed. ✓

## Follow-up exercises

---

**Exercise 1.** Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 2 \\ 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$. Find all eigenvalues and eigenvectors.

**Solution:**

*Characteristic equation:*

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = (3 - \lambda)(4 - \lambda) - 2 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = (\lambda - 5)(\lambda - 2) = 0$$

Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = 5$, $\lambda_2 = 2$.

*For $\lambda_1 = 5$:*

$$A - 5I = \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 2 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \implies v_1 = v_2 \implies \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

*For $\lambda_2 = 2$:*

$$A - 2I = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \implies v_1 = -2v_2 \implies \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

---

**Exercise 2.** Let $C = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 0 \\ 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$. Find all eigenvalues and eigenvectors.

**Solution:**

*Characteristic equation:* Since $C$ is lower triangular, the eigenvalues are the diagonal entries directly:

$$\det(C - \lambda I) = (6 - \lambda)(4 - \lambda) = 0 \implies \lambda_1 = 6, \quad \lambda_2 = 4$$

*For $\lambda_1 = 6$:*

$$C - 6I = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 1 & -2 \end{pmatrix} \implies v_1 = 2v_2 \implies \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

*For $\lambda_2 = 4$:*

$$C - 4I = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \implies 2v_1 = 0 \implies v_1 = 0 \implies \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Exercise 2 is a useful reminder that for **triangular matrices**, the diagonal entries are always the eigenvalues — the row-reduction step is still needed for the eigenvectors, but you can skip the characteristic polynomial entirely.
