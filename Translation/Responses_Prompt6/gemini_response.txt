An endless torrential downpour battered the corrugated tin roof of the temporary command pavilion, creating a deafening symphony of metallic clatter that echoed in the damp air. Inside, the atmosphere was stifling, permeated by the acrid stench of cheap, burnt coffee and the distinctively foul reek of estuary mud. Outside, shrouded in the gray gloom of the storm, the listing skeleton of the "H.M.S. Leviathan" was hopelessly wedged upon the treacherous shoals of the coastal strait. A single tungsten bulb hanging above them swung violently, casting chaotic, pendulum-like shadows across the faces of the two men who had reached a bitter impasse regarding the vessel's salvage operation.
Alistair Vance—a man whose impeccable, bespoke trench coat seemed a deliberate insult to the surrounding squalor—stood frozen by the fogged window. His posture was reminiscent of a classical statue, unwavering and imbued with an ancestral authority. Opposite him, sprawled carelessly on a rusty folding chair, was Jax, whose muddy boots were brazenly propped up on the scratched wooden table. Jax was the supervisor of the motley salvage crew currently engaged in a fruitless attempt to dredge Vance’s multi-million-dollar asset from the murky depths.
— Listen, bro, — Jax mumbled, aggressively chewing on a frayed toothpick. — Long story short? She’s stuck tight. The mud is like glue. I don't have a magic wand to wave and pop her out just like that. If you want her out, we have to cut her. That’s just how it is. My boys are out there in the dark, man. It's pitch black. Total madness. Twisted iron, busted pipes, and mud thick as concrete. You can't just waltz in there and hook up a towline. This needs a gentle touch, you get me? Tact.
Alistair turned slowly, a profound, aristocratic exhaustion etched across his face. "Despite your superficial assessment, the idea of employing destructive measures is entirely unacceptable for our purposes. I find your slang-ridden characterization of this highly delicate engineering endeavor to be exceedingly irritating. The 'Leviathan' is not a simple barge that you can extract from the sludge with a 'gentle touch.' This is a vessel equipped with experimental quantum arrays, the sensitivity of which cannot be overstated. Even the most minute deviation in lateral movement could permanently compromise the optical matrices. I categorically demand that this supplementary proposal of yours be immediately discarded. It is an extremely innovative, yet wholly unacceptable approach."
Jax snorted and dropped the toothpick onto the floorboards. "Discard it? Bro, if we discard it, that means we ain't doing nothing. And what's this about innovative, I ain't reading sci-fi novels here, mate. I'm trying to save your hide. You keep lecturing me on the current situation, but you ain't looking at the actual current. The river's running at ten knots. And the juice from your busted generators? Discharging straight into the water. The whole place is a literal death trap out there."
"Such vivid hyperbole hardly mitigates our fundamental contractual obligations," Alistair replied in an icy tone. "The parameters of our agreement are explicitly codified. Your persistent attempts to characterize a standard maritime salvage operation as a 'death trap' is a dramatic oversimplification of a mathematically solvable hydrostatic equation. I am fully cognizant of the hydrological and electrical realities. However, while standing by the water's edge this morning, I chanced upon a white heron peacefully building its nest on the riverbank. It was entirely unperturbed by the cacophony of the monstrous mechanical crane that you have parked so precariously nearby. And speaking of the bank, my superiors at the Central Bank of London are growing uncharacteristically impatient with your protracted delays."
"Oh, really? Well, tell your suits to come down and take a stroll on this bank," Jax shot back instantly, jabbing a finger vigorously toward the window. "It's pure muck. They'll lose their shiny Italian shoes in two seconds flat. Pumps are failing, hoses are bursting, and the winch is one bad pull away from a catastrophic blowout. We're running on air, duct tape, and spite right now."
Jax leaned forward, planting his boots on the floor with a thud that made the coffee mugs rattle. "Listen, Alistair, dragging this out forever ain't gonna work anymore. We gotta swallow this bitter pill right now and act. There's one massive problem here that we're turning a blind eye to, and that's the fact that your ship is basically snapping in half. Let's cut to the chase. You want the servers pulled out of the hull, but you're too cheap to approve the use of heavy machinery."
Alistair winced and adjusted his wire-rimmed glasses. "Your utilization of colloquial clichés will not advance our mutual enterprise. I have scrutinized the preliminary draft of your revised contract in detail, as well as the architectural blueprints for the containment reservoir. The financial expenditure this demands from our operational reserves is outright extortion, particularly given that, in its current predicament, the vessel has a remarkably shallow draft. Furthermore, standing by this shattered glass, I am being troubled by a dreadful cold draft."
"Then put on a sweater," Jax groaned, rolling his eyes toward the tin roof. "I got thirty men out there freezing to death. I'm not gonna risk them drowning just so you can pinch a few pennies. If you don't approve the hazard pay, I'm going to the union reps and spilling my guts. Then they walk. They'll just throw in the towel, bro, and leave your glowing boxes right in the mud."
Alistair closed his eyes and pinched the bridge of his nose, exhibiting the utmost restraint. "There is no need to remind me of the catastrophic failure we endured in the North Sea two years ago. Corporate leadership dismissed the union representatives, fearing diminished profit margins. Consequently, the operation proceeded with glaring logistical flaws. During the extraction phase, heavy iron chains crushed the wooden holding pallets because they proved too fragile to withstand the kinetic load. I will not permit a repetition of such staggering folly."
Jax shook his head, looking extremely irritated. "Bro, I couldn't care less about the North Sea. I care about what's happening right now. Are you signing the work order or not?"
Alistair’s voice shifted into an ice-cold, authoritative register, laden with bureaucratic dogmatism. "Listen to me very carefully, Jax. Should the international maritime tribunal determine that the structural integrity of the ship's hull was irreparably compromised prior to our intervention today, it is imperative that liability falls squarely upon your syndicate, whose gross negligence has been fully and meticulously documented."
Jax stared blankly. "What did you just say?"
Alistair continued, his tone unwavering. "The principal underwriters have strictly demanded that the retrieval process be immediately halted to prevent a secondary bulkhead rupture. This is an unforeseen contingency which, had it been adequately anticipated by your engineering team, could have been entirely averted through the reinforcement protocols that were, for inexplicable reasons, ignored."
Jax rubbed his temples and let out a humorless laugh. "Your bark is worse than your bite, bro. But hell. You want to talk liability? The bark is peeling off those wooden pilings outside because your ship smashed right into them. We didn't do that. And don't even get me started on that security detail you brought in; the nonstop barking of their guard dogs kept my crew awake all damn night."
Alistair sighed heavily, his voice barely audible against the relentless pounding of the rain. "These waters, this treacherous coastal strait, have proven far more perilous than our initial acoustic soundings indicated. The ambient noise of the storm entirely masks the sound of shrieking steel. I merely require a sound operational strategy, not a reckless, impulsive adventure."
"You want a sound strategy?" Jax stood up and aggressively zipped his reflective jacket all the way to his chin. "Pay the hazard money. Give my boys some motivation. You do that, and we'll rig the pontoons, pump the ballast, and float your precious cargo right to the surface. You don't, and that ship of yours stays on the bottom forever. Your call, boss."
Alistair gazed deeply out the window into the gray, tempestuous abyss, watching the rain lash against the glass. "Although the Board of Directors has repeatedly voiced the opinion that we abandon the sunken cargo to the unforgiving depths, I remain steadfast in my belief that the extraction process must be prioritized, provided that the divers, being adequately informed of the hazardous environment, are guaranteed that even in the event of the operation's catastrophic failure, their exorbitant compensation will be secured."
Jax grinned, his white teeth piercing the dim, shadowy light of the pavilion. "Now you're speaking my language, mate. I'll prep the crew, and they'll be in the water in ten minutes."
"I sincerely doubt that I will ever speak your language, Jax," Alistair muttered, turning his back to the window as the salvage crew supervisor stepped out of the tent and into the roaring storm. "But let us hope, for both our sakes, that this miserable endeavor finally bears fruit."
