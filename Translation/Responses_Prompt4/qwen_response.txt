Negotiations for the Ship's Recovery

The incessant deluge lashed the corrugated iron roof of the temporary command pavilion, creating a deafening symphony of metallic percussion that echoed through the humid air. Inside, the atmosphere was stifling, steeped in the fetid smell of burnt, inferior coffee and the unmistakable stench of estuarine mud. Outside, shrouded in the grey half-light of the storm, the tilted hull of the H.M.S. Leviathan lay hopelessly grounded against the treacherous sandbanks of the coastal strait. A single tungsten bulb swung violently overhead, casting erratic, pendulous shadows across the faces of the two men locked in a bitter stalemate over the salvage operations.

Alistair Vance, a man whose impeccably tailored trench coat seemed a deliberate affront to the surrounding squalor, stood rigid beside the fogged window. His posture was that of a classical statue, inflexible and imbued with ancestral authority. Opposite him, sprawled carelessly on a rusted folding chair with his mud-caked boots brazenly propped upon the scarred wooden table, sat Jax. Jax was the foreman of the ramshackle salvage company that was currently failing to extract Vance's multi-million-pound fortune from the murky waters.

"Look, pal," Jax grumbled, aggressively chewing on a frayed toothpick. "Bottom line? This thing is totally stuck. The mud out there's like glue. There's no magic wand to wave to free it up. If you want her out, we gotta cut her loose. Simple as that. My guys are down there, in the dark, pal. Pitch black, absolute. Out there it's a total mess. There's twisted metal, burst pipes, and mud thick as concrete. You can't just skip in and hook up a tow cable. This needs to be handled with care, y'know? With finesse."

Alistair turned slowly, an expression of profound, aristocratic weariness on his face. "Setting aside your rudimentary assessment, the proposal to employ destructive measures is wholly anathema to our objectives. I find your colloquial characterization of this highly delicate engineering operation extremely irritating. The Leviathan is not some simple barge to be 'handled with kid gloves' to pry it from the silt. It is a vessel equipped with experimental quantum matrices, whose sensitivity cannot be overstated. A mere fraction of a degree in lateral displacement could permanently misalign the optical matrices. I must insist that this supplementary proposal of yours be immediately set aside. It is a decidedly novel approach, yet it remains entirely untenable."

Jax snorted, flicking the toothpick onto the floorboards. "Set it aside? Bro, if we set it aside, we ain't doing jack. And 'novel'? I ain't reading no sci-fi novel here, pal. I'm tryin' to save your ass. You keep preachin' at me about the current situation, but you ain't lookin' at the actual current. The river current out there's runnin' at ten knots. And the electrical current from your busted generators? It's actively electrifyin' the water. It's a bona fide death trap."

"Such vivid hyperbole does little to mitigate the underlying contractual obligations," Alistair replied, his tone glacial. "The parameters of our agreement were explicitly codified. Your insistence on characterizing a standard maritime salvage operation as a 'death trap' is a drastic oversimplification of a hydrostatic equation mathematically resolvable. I am fully aware of the hydrological and electrical realities. However, while I stood aboard the water this morning, I happened to observe a solitary white crane nesting peacefully on the riverbank. The bird remained entirely undisturbed by the cacophony of that monstrous mechanical crane you have parked so precariously nearby. Speaking of the bank, my superiors at the central financial bank in London are becoming unusually impatient regarding your protracted delays."

"Yeah? Well, tell your suits to come down here and take a walk on this bank," Jax shot back, gesturing wildly toward the window. "It's pure mud. They'd get their shiny Italian shoes sucked right in, flat second. I got pumps failin', pipes blowin', and a winch that on a bad pull risks a catastrophic explosion. Right now we're runnin' on fumes, duct tape, and pure rage."

Jax leaned forward, letting his boots drop to the floor with a dull thud that made the coffee cups tremble. "Look, Alistair, we can't keep puttin' this off. We gotta face this head-on, right here, right now. There's a huge elephant in the room, and it's the fact that your boat is literally breakin' in two. Let's cut to the chase. You want the servers out of the hull, but you're too cheap to authorize the heavy-lift equipment."

Alistair stiffened, adjusting his wire-rimmed glasses. "Your recourse to colloquial clichés does absolutely nothing to advance our joint enterprise. I have thoroughly examined the preliminary draft of your revised contract, together with the architectural design of the containment vessel. The financial outlay this would require from our operational reserves is nothing short of extortionate, especially considering the surprisingly shallow draft of the vessel in its current predicament. Furthermore, standing beside this fractured glass, I am catching a rather unpleasant draft."

"Then put on a sweater," Jax groaned, rolling his eyes toward the tin roof. "I got thirty guys out there freezin' their asses off. I ain't about to ask 'em to risk drownin' just so you can save a few bucks. If you don't authorize the hazard pay, I'm goin' public with the union reps. And then they'll strike. They'll throw in the towel, pal, and leave your glow-in-the-dark boxes in the mud."

Alistair closed his eyes, massaging the bridge of his nose in a supreme gesture of self-control. "I need not remind you of the catastrophic fiasco we faced in the North Sea two years ago. Corporate executives dismissed the union representatives because they were terrified of shrinking profit margins. Consequently, the operation proceeded with glaring logistical deficiencies. During the extraction phase, the heavy iron chains crushed the wooden support pallets because they were too fragile to withstand the kinetic load. I will not tolerate the repetition of such staggering ineptitude."

Jax shook his head, an expression of total exasperation on his face. "Pal, I don't give a damn about the North Sea. I care about right now. Sign the work order or don't?"

Alistair's voice dropped to a cold, authoritative register, laden with bureaucratic finality. "Listen to me very carefully, Jax. Should the international maritime tribunal determine that the structural integrity of the hull was irreparably compromised prior to our intervention today, it is imperative that liability be assigned entirely to your consortium, whose flagrant negligence has been meticulously and thoroughly documented."

Jax stared blankly. "Come again?"

Alistair continued, his tone inflexible. "It has been strictly imposed by the principal underwriters that extraction be suspended immediately to prevent failure of the secondary bulkhead—an eventuality which, had it been adequately anticipated by your technical team, would have been entirely averted by the reinforcement protocols that were, inexplicably, discarded."

Jax rubbed his temples, laughing without mirth. "Jeez, your bark is way worse than your bite. But fine. You wanna talk liability? The bark on those wooden pilings out there is peelin' off because your ship rammed right into 'em. That wasn't on us. And don't start in on the security detail you brought; the incessant barkin' of those guard dogs kept my crew awake the whole damn night."

Alistair sighed heavily, a sound barely audible above the relentless drumming of the rain. "This body of water, this insidious coastal strait, has proven far more formidable than our initial acoustic soundings indicated. The ambient noise of the storm is completely masking the groans of the steel. I am simply requesting a sound operational strategy, not a reckless, impulsive gamble."

"You want a sound strategy?" Jax rose, aggressively zipping his high-visibility jacket up to his chin. "Pay the hazard bonus. Let the guys feel they've got skin in the game. You do that, we hook up the pontoons, bleed the ballast, and bring your precious cargo to the surface. You don't, and you're stayin' on the bottom forever. Your call, boss."

Alistair stared intently at the grey, churning abyss beyond the window, watching the rain beat against the glass. "Although it has been repeatedly suggested by the board of directors to abandon the submerged cargo to the inexorable depths, it is my firm conviction that recovery must take priority, provided that the divers, duly informed of the hazardous environment, have assurance that their exorbitant compensation will be guaranteed should the operation be catastrophically compromised."

Jax grinned, a flash of white teeth cutting through the dim, shadowy light of the pavilion. "Now you're speakin' my language, pal. I'll get the team ready and have 'em in the water in ten."

"I sincerely doubt I shall ever manage to speak your language, Jax," Alistair murmured, turning his back to the window as the foreman exited the tent to face the howling storm. "But let us hope, for both our sakes, that this wretched endeavor finally bears fruit."
