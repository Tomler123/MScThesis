The relentless stream of rain hammered against the corrugated tin roof of the temporary command pavilion during the ship salvage negotiations, creating a dull metallic drum symphony that reverberated in the humid air. Inside, the atmosphere was oppressive, thick with the bitter smell of cheap, burnt coffee and the unmistakable stench of estuarine mud. Outside, shrouded in the storm’s gray darkness, the tilted hulk of H.M.S. Leviathan lay endlessly trapped in the treacherous melodies of the coastal sound. A single tungsten bulb swung powerfully overhead, casting irregular, pendulum-like shadows across the faces of the two men locked in the bitter impasse over the salvage operation. 

Alister Vance, the man whose immaculate, tailored trench coat seemed deliberately intended as an insult to the surrounding filth, stood firmly beside the fogged window. His posture was that of a classical statue, unyielding and steeped in ancestral authority. Before him, sprawled carelessly in a rusted folding chair, boots caked in mud propped shamelessly on the damaged wooden table, was Jax. Jax was the foreman of this ragtag salvage crew, currently failing to extract Vance’s multi-million-dollar asset from the murky depths. 

“Look, man,” Jax growled, aggressively gnawing on a broken toothpick. “Bottom line? The thing is super stuck. The mud out there is like glue. I don’t have any magic wand I can wave and free it up. You want it out, we gotta open it. Simple. My boys are down there in the dark, man. Full blackout conditions. It’s total chaos out there. We got twisted metal, busted pipes, and mud thick as concrete. You can’t just go in and hook up a tow line. You gotta coax it, you know? Sweet-talk it.” 

Alister turned slowly, his expression one of profound, aristocratic weariness. “Notwithstanding your elementary assessment, the suggestion of employing destructive measures is utterly anathema to our objectives. I find your colloquial characterization of this exceedingly delicate engineering matter profoundly irritating. The Leviathan is not some mere barge that one ‘coaxes’ out of the mud. It is a vessel equipped with experimental quantum arrays whose sensitivity cannot be overstated. Even a fractional lateral movement could permanently alter the optical matrices. I must insist that we dismiss this additional proposal of yours immediately. It is a highly original approach, yet it remains entirely unacceptable.” 

Jax snorted, flicking the toothpick onto the floorboards. “Dismiss it? Bro, if we dismiss it, we ain’t doing shit. And ‘original’? I ain’t reading sci-fi here, pal. I’m trying to save your ass. You’re lecturing me on the current situation, but you ain’t looking at the real current. Out there the river current is pulling at ten knots. And that electrical current from your busted generators? It’s actively shocking the water. This is a complete death trap.” 

“Such vivid hyperbole does little to alleviate the core contractual obligations,” Alister replied, his tone icy. “The parameters of our agreement were clearly codified. Your insistence on characterizing standard maritime salvage as a ‘death trap’ is a dramatic oversimplification of a mathematically solvable hydrostatic equation. I am acutely aware of the hydrological and electrical realities. However, while standing at the water’s edge this morning, I chanced to notice a solitary white heron nesting peacefully on the riverbank. It remained utterly unperturbed by the cacophony of that monstrous mechanical crane you so hazardously parked nearby. Speaking of the bank, my superiors at the central financial bank in London are losing their uncharacteristic patience with your protracted delays.” 

“Yeah? Well, tell your suits to come down here and take a stroll on this bank,” Jax shot back, gesturing wildly toward the window. “It’s pure mud. Their shiny Italian shoes will be ruined in two seconds. I got pumps that won’t work, hoses that are jamming, and a winch that’s one bad pull away from a catastrophic explosion. We’re running on fumes, duct tape, and spite right now.” 

Jax leaned forward, slamming his boots onto the floor with a heavy thud that rattled the coffee cups. “Look, Alister, we can’t keep kicking this can down the road. We gotta bite the bullet here and now. There’s a giant elephant in the room, and it’s the fact that your boat is literally splitting in two. Let’s cut to the chase. You want the servers out of the hull, but you’re too cheap to authorize the heavy lifting gear.” 

Alister bristled, adjusting his wire-frame glasses. “Your deployment of colloquial clichés does nothing to advance our mutual business. I have studied your revised contract draft in detail, alongside the ship’s architectural plans with parallel containment. The financial projection it demands from our operational reserves is nothing short of extortion, especially given the vessel’s remarkably shallow draft in its current state. Furthermore, standing beside this broken window, I am receiving quite a dreadful cold draft.” 

“Then put on a sweater,” Jax muttered, rolling his eyes toward the tin roof. “I got thirty boys out there freezing their tails off. I’m not gonna ask them to take the risk of diving in just so you can save a few bucks. If you don’t authorize hazard pay, I’ll take it to the union reps. Then they walk. They’ll just quit, man, and leave your fancy boxes in the mud.” 

Alister closed his eyes, pinching the bridge of his nose in a display of supreme restraint. “I require no reminder of the catastrophic collapse we endured in the North Sea two years ago. Corporate executives dismissed the union representatives because they were alarmed by the shrinking profit margins. Accordingly, the operation proceeded with glaring logistical deficiencies. During the extraction phase, the heavy iron chains shattered the wooden support pallets because they were too fragile to withstand the kinetic load. I will not permit a repetition of such spectacular incompetence.” 

Jax shook his head, looking thoroughly exasperated. “Man, I don’t give a damn about the North Sea. I care about right now. You gonna sign that work order or not?” 

Alister’s voice dropped into a cold, authoritative register, laden with bureaucratic finality. “Listen to me very carefully, Jax. If it is determined by an international maritime tribunal that the structural integrity of the hull was irreparably compromised prior to our intervention today, it will be necessary for liability to be transferred entirely to your syndicate, whose gross negligence has been thoroughly and diligently documented.” 

Jax stared blankly. “What are you saying now?” 

Alister continued, his tone unwavering. “It has been rigorously established by the best underwriters that extraction must cease immediately in the event of secondary bulkhead failure—a contingency which, had it been adequately anticipated by your engineering team, would have been entirely preventable through reinforcement protocols that, inexplicably, were destroyed.” 

Jax rubbed his temples, laughing humorlessly. “Man, your bark is way worse than your bite. But fine. You wanna talk liability? Out there the wooden pilings are splintering because your ship plowed straight into them. We didn’t do that. And don’t even start on that security detail you brought; their guard dogs barking nonstop all night kept my crew awake.” 

Alister exhaled sharply, his voice barely audible above the relentless drum of the rain. “This body of water, this treacherous coastal sound, proved far stronger than our initial acoustic soundings indicated. The ambient noise of the storm completely masks the steel’s groaning. I require only a solid operational strategy, not reckless, impulsive risk.” 

“You want a solid strategy?” Jax stood, aggressively zipping his high-visibility jacket up to the collar. “Pay the hazard rate. Give my boys something to play for. You do that, we’ll reinforce the pontoons, blow the ballast, and float your precious cargo to the surface. No, and it stays on the bottom forever. Your call, boss.” 

Alister gazed deeply into the gray, swirling abyss beyond the window, watching the rain lash the glass. “Notwithstanding the board of directors’ repeated suggestions that the sunken cargo should be abandoned in the perpetual depths, it is my firm conviction that extraction must remain the priority—provided the divers, who are sufficiently informed about the hazardous environment, are assured that their supplemental compensation will be guaranteed in the event the operation ends catastrophically.” 

Jax grinned, a flash of white teeth piercing the pavilion’s dim, shadowed light. “Now you’re speaking my language, pal. I’ll have the team ready and in the water in ten minutes.” 

“I sincerely doubt I shall ever speak your language, Jax,” Alister whispered, turning his back to the window as the salvage foreman stepped out of the tent into the howling storm. “But we may hope, for both our sakes, that this wretched endeavor finally bears fruit.”
