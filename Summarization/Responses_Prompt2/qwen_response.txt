**Purpose**
The purpose of this research is to align large language models with user intent by fine-tuning them with human feedback. Large language models often express unintended behaviors such as making up facts, generating biased or toxic text, or simply not following user instructions. This work seeks to demonstrate that fine-tuning with human feedback is a promising direction for aligning language models with human intent.

**Method**
Starting with labeler-written prompts, the authors collect a dataset of demonstrations to fine-tune GPT-3 using supervised learning. They then collect a dataset of rankings of model outputs to train a reward model which predicts human-preferred output. Finally, they use this reward model to fine-tune the supervised baseline using reinforcement learning from human feedback via the PPO algorithm, calling the resulting models InstructGPT.

**Key Contributions**
The study shows that a 1.3B parameter InstructGPT model is preferred over the 175B GPT-3 model despite having 100x fewer parameters. InstructGPT models demonstrate improvements in truthfulness and reductions in toxic output generation while maintaining minimal performance regressions on public NLP datasets. Additionally, the paper identifies a method to minimize these regressions by mixing pretraining updates with PPO updates during the fine-tuning procedure.

**Results**
On their test set, outputs from the 175B InstructGPT model are preferred to outputs from 175B GPT-3 85 ± 3% of the time. On the TruthfulQA benchmark, InstructGPT generates truthful and informative answers about twice as often as GPT-3, and hallucination rates are halved on closed-domain tasks. While toxicity improves by about 25% under respectful prompts, the models do not significantly improve over GPT-3 on standard bias datasets like Winogender.

**Limitations**
InstructGPT still makes simple mistakes, such as failing to detect instructions with false premises or providing long hedging answers to simple questions. The models may follow user instructions even if that leads to harm, meaning they are neither fully aligned nor fully safe. The alignment is based on the preferences of specific labelers and researchers, necessitating further work to generalize to broader groups of users.
