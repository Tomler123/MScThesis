Negotiations over the Salvage of the Ship

The unceasing downpour hammered the corrugated tin roof of the temporary command pavilion, creating a deafening symphony of metallic clatter that reverberated through the damp air. Inside, the atmosphere was suffocating, steeped in the acrid smell of cheap, burnt coffee and the unmistakable stench of estuary mud. Outside, wrapped in the gray blur of the torrent, the listing hulk of H.M.S. Leviathan was hopelessly jammed against the treacherous shoals of the coastal inlet. The single tungsten bulb hanging overhead swung furiously, carrying confused, pendulum-like shadows across the faces of two men trapped in a bitter deadlock over the salvage operation.

Alistair Vance, a man whose impeccably stitched, bespoke trench coat seemed to be deliberately insulting the filth reigning all around, stood as though frozen by the fogged-up window. His posture resembled that of a classical statue—immovable and saturated with ancestral authority. Opposite him, sprawled carelessly in a rusted folding chair, boots smeared with mud insolently planted on the scarred wooden table, sat Jax. Jax was the supervisor of the ragtag salvage crew that was currently failing to drag Vance’s multimillion-dollar asset up from the murky depths.

“Listen, brother,” muttered Jax, aggressively chewing a worn toothpick. “Here’s the main thing: that damn thing is stuck hard. The mud down there is like glue. I ain’t got no magic wand to wave and yank it loose just like that. You want it brought up? Then we gotta bust it open. That’s all there is to it. My boys are down there, in the dark, brother. Pitch black. Total fucking nightmare down there. We got twisted iron, burst pipes, and mud thick as concrete. You can’t just stroll in and hook on a towline. You gotta work it, got it? Ease into it.”

Alistair turned slowly; deep, aristocratic fatigue was written across his face. “Despite your primitive assessment, the proposal to employ destructive measures is entirely anathema to our objectives. I find your colloquial characterization of this exceptionally delicate engineering process profoundly irritating. Leviathan is not merely a barge to be ‘worked’ out of the silt. It is a vessel equipped with experimental quantum arrays whose sensitivity cannot be overstated. Even the slightest fraction of a degree of lateral displacement could permanently throw the optical matrices out of alignment. I must insist that this supplemental proposal of yours be removed from the agenda at once. It is a thoroughly unconventional approach, and remains wholly unacceptable.”

Jax snorted and flicked the toothpick onto the planks. “Remove it from the agenda? Brother, if we remove it, we ain’t doing a damn thing at all. And ‘unconventional’? I’m not reading a science-fiction novel here, pal. I’m saving your hide. You keep lecturing me about current, but you’re not even looking at the actual current. The river’s pulling at ten knots out there. And that power leaking from your busted generators? It’s hitting the water live. It’s a straight-up death trap.”

“Such conspicuous hyperbole does nothing to diminish the obligations assumed under contract,” Alistair replied in an icy tone. “The parameters of our agreement were codified with perfect clarity. Your insistence on christening a routine marine-salvage operation a ‘death trap’ amounts to a dramatic simplification of a mathematically solvable hydrostatic equation. I am fully cognizant of the hydrological and electrical realities. However, when I stood at the water’s edge this morning, I happened to notice a solitary white crane serenely nesting along the riverbank. It appeared entirely untroubled by the cacophony of that monstrous mechanical crane which you have parked so precariously nearby. And since I mention the bank, my superiors at the Central Financial Bank of London are becoming unusually impatient with your protracted delays.”

“Oh yeah? Then tell your suited lot to come down here and take a stroll along that bank,” shot back Jax, sweeping a broad hand toward the window. “It’s pure sludge. Their shiny Italian shoes’d sink in two seconds. My pumps are breaking down, my hoses are splitting, and one of the crane winches is one bad pull away from a catastrophic blowout. Right now we’re operating on fumes, duct tape, and stubbornness.”

Jax leaned forward and dropped his boots to the floor with a heavy bang that rattled the coffee cups. “Listen, Alistair, we can’t keep kicking the can down the road like this. We gotta bite that bitter pill right here, right now. There’s a huge elephant in the room, and that is your boat’s practically breaking in half. Let’s get right to the point. You want the servers pulled out of the hull, but you’re so damn stingy you won’t authorize the heavy crane gear.”

Alistair frowned and adjusted his wire-rimmed spectacles. “Your use of colloquial clichés does nothing whatsoever to advance our common undertaking. I have studied with great care the preliminary draft of your revised contract, as well as the architectural design for the containment vessel. The financial projection this would require from our operational reserves amounts, quite simply, to extortion, especially given the vessel’s surprisingly shallow draft in its present condition. Furthermore, as I stand beside this shattered pane, I find myself subjected to a rather distressing cold draft.”

“Then put on a sweater,” groaned Jax, rolling his eyes toward the tin roof. “I got thirty men out there freezing their asses off. I’m not about to ask them to risk drowning just because you wanna save a few pennies. If you don’t approve the hazardous-conditions add-on, I’ll tell the union reps everything. Then they’ll walk. They’ll just throw up their hands, brother, and leave your glowing boxes in the mud.”

Alistair closed his eyes and pressed his fingers to the bridge of his nose, in the highest expression of restraint. “Surely I need not remind you of the catastrophic fiasco we endured in the North Sea two years ago. The corporate executives dismissed the union representatives for fear of reducing profit margins. As a result, the operation proceeded with glaring logistical deficiencies. During the lifting phase, the heavy iron chains shattered the wooden support pallets because they were far too fragile to withstand the kinetic load. I cannot tolerate a repetition of such staggering incompetence.”

Jax shook his head, looking utterly exasperated. “Brother, I don’t give a damn about the North Sea. I care about what’s happening right now. You signing the work order or not?”

Alistair’s voice went cold and assumed a register laden with commanding, bureaucratic finality. “Listen to me very carefully, Jax. If the International Maritime Tribunal determines that the structural integrity of the hull had already been irreparably compromised before our intervention today, then it is absolutely imperative that liability be assigned in its entirety to your syndicate, whose manifest negligence has been thoroughly and meticulously documented.”

Jax stared blankly. “What the hell did you just say?”

Alistair continued, his tone implacable. “The lead insurers have sternly insisted that the salvage be halted immediately so as to prevent the collapse of the secondary watertight bulkhead—a circumstance that would have been entirely avoidable had it been properly anticipated by your engineering team through the reinforcement protocols which, inexplicably, were rejected.”

Jax rubbed his temples and gave a reluctant laugh. “Man, your bark’s a whole lot worse than your bite. But fine. You wanna talk liability? The bark’s peeling off those wooden pilings out there because your ship slammed right into them. We didn’t do that. And don’t even get me started on the security people you brought in; their watchdogs kept barking all night and my crew didn’t get a wink of sleep.”

Alistair exhaled heavily; the sound was barely audible against the relentless hammering of the rain. “This body of water, this treacherous coastal inlet, has proved far more formidable than our initial acoustic sounding indicated. The external noise of the storm entirely masks the groaning of the steel. What I require is only an operationally sound strategy, not reckless, impulsive bravado.”

“You want a sound strategy?” Jax rose to his feet and yanked his high-visibility jacket aggressively up to his neck. “Pay the hazardous-conditions rate. Give my boys some incentive. You do that, we’ll lash on the pontoons, blow the ballast, and float your precious cargo to the surface. You don’t, and it stays on the bottom forever. Your call, boss.”

Alistair gazed for a long time into the gray, raging abyss beyond the window, watching the rain lash the glass. “Although the board of directors has repeatedly proposed abandoning the submerged cargo to the merciless depths, it remains my firm conviction that its recovery must remain the priority, on the condition that the divers, having been duly informed of the perilous environment, be guaranteed confirmation of their exorbitant compensation in the event that the operation collapses catastrophically.”

Jax smiled; his white teeth flashed through the dim, shadow-filled light of the pavilion. “There, now you’re speaking my language, pal. I’ll prep the crew and they’ll be in the water in ten minutes.”

“I sincerely doubt I shall ever speak your language, Jax,” murmured Alistair, turning his back to the window as the salvage supervisor stepped out of the tent into the screaming storm. “But let us hope, for both our sakes, that this wretched enterprise will at long last bear fruit.”
