**Purpose**
The paper aims to address the misalignment between large language models and user intent, particularly issues like untruthful, toxic, or unhelpful outputs. It proposes a method to align models with human intentions—defined as being helpful, honest, and harmless—using human feedback. The goal is to improve instruction-following behavior across a wide range of natural language tasks. The work introduces InstructGPT as a case study of this alignment approach.

**Method**
The method uses reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) in a three-step pipeline: supervised fine-tuning (SFT), reward model (RM) training, and reinforcement learning via proximal policy optimization (PPO). First, labelers provide demonstrations of desired outputs to fine-tune a pretrained GPT-3 model. Second, a reward model is trained on human rankings of model outputs to predict preferences. Finally, the model is optimized with PPO using the reward model as a scalar reward signal, optionally mixed with pretraining gradients (PPO-ptx) to reduce performance regressions.

**Key Contributions**
The paper demonstrates that fine-tuning with human feedback can significantly improve alignment with user intent compared to scaling model size alone. It introduces the InstructGPT models, which outperform larger GPT-3 models in human preference evaluations despite having fewer parameters. The work shows that RLHF improves truthfulness and reduces toxic outputs while maintaining general performance. It also highlights that alignment can generalize to unseen tasks and user instructions beyond the training distribution.

**Results**
Human evaluators preferred outputs from the 1.3B InstructGPT model over the 175B GPT-3 model, showing strong gains in alignment efficiency. The models improved truthfulness, roughly doubling performance on TruthfulQA and reducing hallucination rates in closed-domain tasks (21% vs. 41%). Toxicity was reduced by about 25% in certain prompted settings, though bias metrics showed no significant improvement. Performance regressions on some public NLP benchmarks were mitigated using PPO-ptx without sacrificing alignment gains.

**Limitations**
The alignment is dependent on a small, non-representative group of human labelers, whose preferences may not generalize to broader populations. The models still produce biased, toxic, or incorrect outputs and can fail on tasks with false premises or complex constraints. There is an “alignment tax,” where performance on some benchmarks decreases during RLHF fine-tuning. Additionally, the approach aligns models to specific human preferences rather than universal values, raising questions about fairness and general applicability.
