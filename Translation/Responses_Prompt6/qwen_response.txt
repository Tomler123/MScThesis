Negotiations Regarding the Ship’s Recovery

The relentless deluge battered the corrugated tin roof of the temporary command pavilion, creating a grating symphony that reverberated with metallic percussion through the humid air. Inside, the atmosphere was suffocating, saturated with the acrid scent of cheap, burnt coffee and the unmistakable sharpness of estuarine mud. Outside, shrouded in the grey haze of a freezing drizzle, the slanted skeleton of the H.M.S. *Leviathan* lay hopelessly wedged among the treacherous shoals of the coastal strait. A single tungsten bulb swayed violently from the ceiling, swinging like a pendulum and casting unstable shadows across the faces of two men trapped in a bitter impasse over the salvage operation.

Alistair Vance, a man whose immaculate, bespoke trench coat seemed a deliberate affront to the surrounding filth, stood rigidly before the fogged window. His posture recalled a classical sculpture—unyielding and steeped in ancestral authority. Opposite him, sprawled carelessly upon a rusted folding chair, sat Jax. His mud-caked boots were perched arrogantly on a scarred wooden table. Jax was the head of this ragtag, heterogeneous salvage crew, currently failing to raise Vance’s multimillion-dollar asset from the misty depths.

“Long story short, man,” Jax spat out, aggressively chewing on a frayed toothpick. “The main thing? The thing’s stuck tight. The mud down there’s like glue. I don’t got a magic wand to yank it out with a flick of my wrist. You want it out, we gotta take it apart. That’s it. My boys are down there in the dark, man. Can’t make out a damn thing. It’s total chaos. Tangled metal, busted pipes, mud hard as concrete. You can’t just step in there and clip a tow cable. You gotta work it, you feel me? Gotta be gentle.”

Alistair turned slowly, his face etched with profound, aristocratic exhaustion. “Notwithstanding your elementary assessment, the proposal to employ destructive measures is entirely unacceptable to our objectives. Your colloquial characterization of this exceedingly delicate engineering process is profoundly irritating. The *Leviathan* is not some mere barge to be ‘gently lifted’ from the mud. This is a vessel outfitted with experimental quantum systems of immeasurable sensitivity. Even the slightest lateral deviation will permanently compromise the optical matrices. I demand that this additional proposal of yours be tabled immediately. It is, one might say, novel, yet entirely unjustified.”

Jax let out a cynical chuckle and tossed his toothpick to the floor. “Table it? Bro, if we table it, we do nothing. And ‘novel’? I ain’t writing a science fiction novel here, mister. I’m saving your skin. You keep asking me for status updates, but you’re blind to the real flow. The current’s pushing ten knots out there. And the electrical leakage from your busted generators? Drops straight into the water. It’s a full-blown death trap down there.”

“Such stark hyperbole can in no way mitigate the obligations stipulated by contract,” Alistair replied, his voice frozen. “The terms of our agreement have been clearly codified. Characterizing standard maritime salvage operations as a ‘death trap’ constitutes a gross oversimplification of a mathematically solvable hydrostatic equation. I possess absolute clarity regarding the hydrological and electrical realities. However, while standing at the water’s edge this morning, I happened to observe a solitary white heron nesting peacefully by the riverbank. It remained entirely unfazed by the intolerable din of that monstrous mechanical crane you so hazardously positioned nearby. On the subject of the shoreline, I must inform you that my superiors at the London Central Financial Bank are exceedingly displeased with your protracted delays.”

“Yeah, sure? What else, tell your suit-clad boys to come down here and walk this bank themselves,” Jax shot back sharply, waving a hand angrily toward the window. “It’s pure swamp. Their shiny Italian shoes would sink in two seconds. My pumps are burning out, hoses are snapping, and the lifting rig will fail catastrophically from one bad tug. We’re holding on by fumes, duct tape, and stubbornness right now.”

Jax leaned forward, his boots hitting the floor with a heavy thud that rattled the coffee cups. “Listen, Alistair, we’re stalling. We need to take the bitter pill here and now. There’s an elephant in the room, and it’s that your ship is literally splitting in half. Let’s get to the point. You want the servers pulled from the hull, but you won’t cough up the cash to secure heavy-lift equipment.”

A shiver ran through Alistair as he adjusted his wire-framed glasses. “Your recourse to everyday clichés does nothing to advance our shared endeavor. I have covertly reviewed your preliminary draft of the amended contract, alongside the architectural schematics for the internal container. The financial burden you demand from our operational reserve borders on banking extortion, particularly given that the vessel’s draft in this condition is remarkably minimal. Furthermore, standing by this broken window, I am detecting a rather sharp draft.”

“Then put on a sweater,” Jax scoffed, rolling his eyes toward the tin roof. “I’ve got thirty men out there freezing to the bone. I ain’t taking the risk of drowning them so you can save a few bucks. If you don’t pay the hazard rate, I’m spilling everything to the union reps. Then it’s over. They’ll just walk, man, and leave your glowing boxes in the mud.”

Alistair closed his eyes and pressed his fingers to the bridge of his nose, a demonstration of supreme restraint. “It is unnecessary to remind you of the catastrophic failure we endured in the North Sea two years ago. Corporate executives disregarded union representatives out of excessive fear for diminished profits. Consequently, the operation proceeded with glaring logistical deficiencies. During the recovery phase, heavy iron chains shattered the wooden retaining pallets, as they proved insufficiently robust to withstand kinetic load. You cannot permit a repetition of such colossal ignorance.”

Jax shook his head, his expression thoroughly disillusioned. “Bro, I don’t care about the North Sea. I only care about this minute. You signing the work order or not?”

Alistair’s voice dropped to a frozen, authoritative tone, saturated with bureaucratic finality. “Listen carefully, Jax. If the International Maritime Tribunal determines that the hull’s structural integrity was already irreparably compromised prior to our intervention, liability must fully transfer to your syndicate, whose apparent negligence has been thoroughly and meticulously documented.”

Jax stared back with a bewildered, empty gaze. “What now?”

Alistair continued, his tone uncompromising. “The primary insurers have strictly demanded that salvage operations be ceased immediately before the intermediate bulkhead collapses. This circumstance, which your engineering team would have duly accounted for, would have been entirely prevented by the reinforcement protocols that were, inexplicably, disregarded.”

Jax rubbed his temples and let out a silent chuckle. “Man, your bark is way worse than your bite. But fine. You wanna talk liability? The bark on those wooden pilings out there is peeling off in strips because your ship drove right into them. We didn’t do that. And don’t even get me started on the security details you brought; their guard dogs’ nonstop barking kept me up all night.”

Alistair drew a deep breath, his voice nearly vanishing into the relentless drumming of the rain. “This aquatic expanse, this treacherous coastal strait, has proven far more severe than our initial acoustic measurements suggested. Background storm sounds completely mask the groaning of the steel. I demand only a coherent operational strategy, not reckless, impulsive gambling.”

“You want a coherent strategy?” Jax stood up and zipped his high-visibility jacket to his chin. “Pay the hazard rate. Cut my boys in on the risk. If you do, we’ll secure the pontoons, vent the ballast, and float your precious cargo to the surface. If not, it stays on the bottom forever. Your call, boss.”

Alistair fixed his gaze on the gloomy, grey abyss beyond the window, watching the rain streak the glass. “Although the board of directors has repeatedly suggested abandoning the sunken cargo to the endless depths, I am firmly convinced that recovery must remain the priority, on the condition that the divers, once adequately apprised of the hazardous environment, are guaranteed that their elevated compensation will be fully secured even should the operation end in catastrophic failure.”

A grin spread across Jax’s face, white teeth flashing in the pavilion’s dim, shadowed light. “Now you’re speaking my language, bro. I’ll prep the team in ten and send ‘em in.”

“I strongly doubt I shall ever speak your language, Jax,” Alistair murmured slowly, turning his back to the window as the salvage operation’s director strode out into the howling storm. “Nevertheless, in the interest of both our parties, I hope this desperate endeavor ultimately yields results.”
