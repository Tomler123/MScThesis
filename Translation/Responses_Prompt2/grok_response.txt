Negotiations on the Salvage of the Ship

The relentless downpour drummed against the corrugated tin roof of the temporary command pavilion, creating a deafening symphony of metallic clattering that echoed in the humid air. Inside, the atmosphere was suffocating, thick with the acrid smell of cheap, scorched coffee and the distinctive foul stench of the estuary mud. Outside, shrouded in the gray gloom of the squall, the listing hulk of H.M.S. Leviathan lay hopelessly stranded on the treacherous shoals of the coastal bay. A single tungsten lightbulb swung furiously overhead, casting chaotic, pendulum-like shadows across the faces of the two men locked in a bitter stalemate over the salvage operation.

Alister Vance, a man whose impeccable, custom-tailored trench coat seemed a deliberate affront to the surrounding filth, stood motionless by the fogged window. His posture was that of a classical statue, unyielding and steeped in ancestral authority. Opposite him, slouched casually in a rusty folding chair with mud-caked boots insolently propped on the battered wooden table, was Jax. Jax was the foreman of a motley salvage crew that was currently unsuccessfully attempting to extract Vance’s multimillion-dollar asset from the murky depths.

“Listen up, man,” muttered Jax, aggressively chewing a frayed toothpick. “Bottom line? This thing is stuck solid. The mud down there is like glue. I don’t have any magic wand I can wave to yank her out. You want to pull her out, you’re gonna have to cut her up. It’s that simple. My boys are down there in the dark, man. Total blackout. It’s a complete fucking clusterfuck down there. We’ve got twisted metal, busted pipes, and mud thick as concrete. You can’t just barge in and hook up a tow cable. You gotta massage it, you get me? With finesse.”

Alister slowly turned, his face bearing an expression of profound, aristocratic weariness. “Notwithstanding your primitive conclusion, the proposal to employ destructive measures is wholly at odds with our objectives. I find your colloquial description of this exceedingly delicate engineering endeavor extremely irritating. The ‘Leviathan’ is not some ordinary barge that can be ‘massaged’ out of the silt. It is a vessel equipped with experimental quantum arrays, whose sensitivity cannot be overstated. A mere fraction of a degree in lateral shift could permanently disrupt the alignment of the optical matrices. I must insist that we immediately set aside this additional proposal of yours. It is an extraordinarily innovative approach; however, it remains entirely unacceptable.”

Jax snorted, tossing the toothpick onto the floorboards. “Set aside? Bro, if we set it aside, we’re doing nothing. And ‘innovative’? I’m not reading some sci-fi novel here, pal. I’m trying to save your ass. You keep lecturing me about the current situation, but you’re not looking at the real current. The river current out there is ripping at ten knots. And the electrical current from your busted generators? It’s actively electrifying the water. It’s a total death trap.”

“Such vivid hyperbole does little to mitigate the underlying contractual obligations,” replied Alister in an icy tone. “The parameters of our agreement were clearly codified. Your insistence on describing standard maritime salvage as a ‘death trap’ is a dramatic oversimplification of a mathematically solvable hydrostatic equation. I am perfectly apprised of the hydrological and electrical realities. However, when I stood this morning at the water’s edge, I happened to observe a solitary white crane peacefully nesting on the riverbank. It remained utterly unperturbed by the cacophony of that monstrous mechanical crane which you parked so precariously nearby. Speaking of the bank, my superiors at the central financial bank in London are growing extraordinarily impatient with your protracted delays.”

“Yeah? Well, tell your suits to come down here and take a walk along this shore,” snarled Jax, gesturing wildly toward the window. “It’s pure swamp out there. They’d lose their shiny Italian shoes in two seconds flat. My pumps are failing, hoses are bursting, and the winch is one bad yank away from a catastrophic explosion. We’re running on fumes, duct tape, and sheer rage right now.”

Jax leaned forward, dropping his boots to the floor with a heavy thud that made the coffee mugs rattle. “Listen, Alister, we can’t keep kicking the can down the road. We need to grit our teeth right here and now. There’s a huge elephant in the room, and the fact is your boat is literally breaking in half. Let’s get to the point. You want the servers out of the hull, but you’re too cheap to authorize heavy lifting equipment.”

Alister bristled, adjusting his wire-rimmed glasses. “Your use of colloquial clichés advances nothing in our joint enterprise. I have carefully reviewed the preliminary draft of your revised contract along with the architectural blueprint of the container vessel. The financial draft that this would require from our operating reserves is nothing short of extortionate, especially given the surprisingly shallow draft of the vessel in its current position. Moreover, standing beside this shattered glass, I am catching a rather dreadful cold draft.”

“Then put on a sweater,” groaned Jax, rolling his eyes toward the tin roof. “I’ve got thirty guys out there freezing their asses off. I’m not gonna ask them to risk drowning just so you can save a few bucks. If you don’t approve the hazard pay, I’ll spill everything to the union reps. Then they’ll walk. They’ll just throw in the towel, man, and leave your glowing boxes in the mud.”

Alister closed his eyes, pinching the bridge of his nose in the utmost display of restraint. “I need not remind you of the catastrophic failure we endured in the North Sea two years ago. The corporate executives rejected the union representatives because they feared profit reductions. As a result, the operation proceeded with egregious logistical deficiencies. During the extraction phase, heavy iron chains crushed the wooden support pallets because they were too fragile to withstand the kinetic load. I will not tolerate a repetition of such flagrant incompetence.”

Jax shook his head, looking utterly exhausted. “Dude, I don’t give a shit about the North Sea. What matters is right now. Are you signing the work order or not?”

Alister’s voice dropped to an icy, authoritative tone laced with bureaucratic finality. “Listen to me very carefully, Jax. If the international maritime tribunal determines that the structural integrity of the hull was irreparably compromised prior to our intervention today, it is of the utmost importance that all liability be shifted entirely onto your syndicate, whose flagrant negligence has been meticulously and painstakingly documented.”

Jax stared blankly. “What now?”

Alister continued, his tone unyielding. “This was strictly mandated by the lead underwriters, that the extraction be immediately suspended so as to prevent the failure of the secondary bulkhead, an eventuality which, had it been properly anticipated by your engineering team, would have been entirely prevented by the reinforcement protocols which, inexplicably, were discarded.”

Jax rubbed his temples, laughing mirthlessly. “Man, your bark is way worse than your bite. But fine. You wanna talk responsibility? The bark on those wooden pilings out there is peeling because your ship plowed straight into them. We didn’t do that. And don’t even start about the security detail you brought in; the nonstop barking of their guard dogs kept my crew up all fucking night.”

Alister heaved a heavy sigh, the sound barely audible over the relentless drumbeat of the rain. “This body of water, this treacherous coastal bay, has proven far more formidable than our initial acoustic soundings indicated. The background noise of the storm completely drowns out the groans of the steel. All I need is a reliable operational strategy, not a reckless, impulsive gamble.”

“You want a reliable strategy?” Jax stood up, aggressively zipping up his high-visibility jacket all the way to his chin. “Pay the risk premium. Give my guys a stake in the game. Do that—we’ll secure the pontoons, blow the ballast, and raise your precious cargo to the surface. Don’t—and it stays on the bottom forever. Your call, boss.”

Alister gazed deeply into the gray, churning abyss beyond the window, watching as the rain lashed the glass. “Although the board of directors has repeatedly suggested abandoning the sunken cargo in the unrelenting depths, I am firmly convinced that the extraction must remain a priority, provided that the divers, who have been duly informed of the hazardous conditions, are assured that their excessive remuneration will be guaranteed in the event that the operation suffers a catastrophic failure.”

Jax grinned, flashing white teeth in the dim, shadowy light of the pavilion. “Now you’re speaking my language, pal. I’ll get the crew ready and send them into the water in ten minutes.”

“I sincerely doubt that I will ever speak your language, Jax,” muttered Alister, turning his back to the window as the salvage foreman exited the tent into the howling storm. “But let us hope, for both our sakes, that this wretched enterprise finally bears fruit.”
