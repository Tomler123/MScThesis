Salvage Negotiations

The relentless downpour lashed the corrugated tin roof of the temporary command pavilion, producing a deafening symphony of metallic din that carried through the damp air. Inside, the atmosphere was suffocating — thick, saturated with the acrid smell of cheap burnt coffee and the ineradicable stench of estuarine silt. Outside, concealed by the grey murk of the squall, the hopelessly grounded wreck of the *H.M.S. Leviathan* sat jammed fast in the treacherous shallows of the coastal strait. A solitary tungsten bulb swung furiously from the ceiling, casting wild, pendulum-like shadows across the faces of two men locked in implacable confrontation over the salvage operation.

Alistair Vance — a man whose immaculate bespoke trench coat seemed a deliberate affront to the surrounding squalor — stood upright by the fogged window. His bearing was that of an antique statue: unyielding, steeped in dynastic authority. Opposite him, sprawled carelessly in a rusted folding chair with his mud-caked boots insolently propped on the scarred wooden table, sat Jaxx — foreman of the motley salvage crew that had so far failed to extract Vance's multi-million-pound asset from the murky depths.

"Look, mate," Jaxx muttered, gnawing furiously at a frayed toothpick. "Bottom line: she's stuck solid. The mud's pure glue. I haven't got a magic wand to yank her out. You want her up — you've got to cut her open. Simple as that. My guys are down there in the dark, mate. Zero visibility. It's a complete shit-show. Twisted metal, burst pipes, mud like concrete. You don't just waltz in and hook up a towline. You've got to work smart, yeah? Surgical."

Alistair turned slowly, with an expression of profound, aristocratic fatigue. "Notwithstanding your reductive assessment, the application of destructive methods is entirely contrary to our objectives. I find your colloquial characterisation of this supremely delicate engineering undertaking to be in the highest degree irritating. The *Leviathan* is not some common barge to be 'surgically' prised from the silt. This is a vessel equipped with experimental quantum matrices whose sensitivity cannot be overstated. The slightest horizontal deviation of a fraction of a degree is capable of irreversibly misaligning the optical arrays. I insist that we remove your supplementary proposal from consideration immediately. It is, admittedly, possessed of a certain originality, yet it remains wholly unacceptable."

Jaxx snorted and flung the toothpick to the floor. "Remove it from consideration? Brother, if we remove it, we're not doing anything at all. And 'originality'? I'm not writing a bloody novel here, pal. I'm trying to save your skin. You keep going on about the 'current state of affairs,' but you won't look at the actual current. Out there, the river's pushing ten knots. And the electrical current from your dead generators? It's cooking the water as we speak. It's a genuine death trap."

"Such vivid hyperbole does nothing whatsoever to alleviate the burden of the contractual obligations underpinning our agreement," replied Alistair in an icy tone. "The parameters of our contract were explicitly enshrined. Your persistent insistence on portraying standard maritime salvage operations as a 'death trap' constitutes a dramatic oversimplification of a mathematically soluble hydrostatic equation. I am fully cognisant of the hydrological and electrical realities. However, this very morning, standing at the water's edge, I happened upon a solitary white heron, peacefully settled upon its nest on the riverbank. The bird remained entirely serene, unmoved by the cacophony produced by that monstrous mechanical crane which you so recklessly positioned nearby. Speaking of banks: my directors at the central financial bank in London are displaying mounting impatience regarding your protracted delays."

"Yeah? Well tell your suits to come down here and take a stroll along that bank," Jaxx snapped, waving furiously toward the window. "Pure bog. They'd lose their shiny Italian shoes in two seconds flat. I've got pumps failing, hoses splitting, and the winch is one good tug away from blowing to hell. We're running on fumes, duct tape, and spite."

Jaxx leaned forward, slamming his boots to the floor with a resounding crack that rattled the coffee mugs. "Listen, Alistair, we can't keep dragging this out forever. We need to grab the bull by the horns right here and now. There's a bloody great elephant in the room, and its name is the fact that your tub is literally breaking in half. No beating around the bush. You want to pull the servers from the hold, but you're baulking at authorising the heavy lift equipment."

Alistair bristled and adjusted his wire-framed spectacles. "Your deployment of hackneyed colloquial clichés does nothing to advance our mutual enterprise. I have studied closely the preliminary draft of your revised contract, as well as the construction schematic for the container vessel. The financial extraction from our operating reserves that it demands is nothing short of extortion, particularly given the surprisingly shallow draught of the vessel in its current predicament. Furthermore, standing beside that broken window frame, I have caught a dreadful draught and fear I shall be seriously ill."

"Then put a jumper on," Jaxx groaned, rolling his eyes toward the tin ceiling. "I've got thirty men freezing out there. I'm not going to ask them to risk their lives so you can save a penny. You don't sign off on the hazard pay — I go straight to the union reps. Then they walk. Just drop everything, mate, and leave your glowing boxes to rot in the mud."

Alistair closed his eyes and pinched the bridge of his nose — the supreme expression of restraint. "I have no doubt you require no reminder of the catastrophic failure we suffered in the North Sea two years ago. The corporate directors dismissed the union representatives, alarmed by the shrinking profit margin. As a result, the operation proceeded with egregious logistical deficiencies. During the lift, heavy iron chains crushed the wooden pallets, which proved entirely unequal to such kinetic loading. I have no intention of tolerating a repetition of such monstrous incompetence."

Jaxx shook his head in a portrait of utter despair. "Mate, I don't give a damn about the North Sea. I care about what's happening right now. Are you signing the work order or not?"

Alistair's voice dropped to a cold, commanding register, weighted with bureaucratic finality. "Listen to me very carefully, Jaxx. Should the international maritime tribunal determine that the structural integrity of the hull was irreversibly compromised prior to our intervention today, it is absolutely essential that liability be placed entirely upon your syndicate, whose egregious negligence has been comprehensively and meticulously documented."

Jaxx stared at him blankly. "Come again?"

Alistair continued, unrelenting. "The lead underwriters have categorically prescribed the immediate suspension of the lift to prevent the destruction of the secondary bulkhead — an outcome which, had your engineering team possessed sufficient foresight, could have been entirely avoided through reinforcement protocols that were, inexplicably, rejected."

Jaxx rubbed his temples and let out a short laugh — without a trace of humour. "Mate, your bark is a hell of a lot worse than your bite. Right. You want to talk liability? The bark is coming off those wooden pilings outside because your rust bucket drove straight into them. We didn't do that. And don't even get me started on that security detail you brought in — my crew didn't sleep a wink all bloody night for the barking of their guard dogs."

Alistair exhaled heavily — the sound barely audible through the unrelenting roar of the rain. "This body of water, this treacherous coastal strait, has proven a far more formidable obstacle than our initial acoustic soundings indicated. The noise of the storm drowns out the groan of the metal entirely. All I require is a rational operational strategy — not some reckless, impulsive gamble."

"You want a rational strategy?" Jaxx leapt to his feet, sharply zipping his hi-vis jacket up to the chin. "Pay the hazard bonus. Give my guys a stake in the job. You do that — we strap on the pontoons, blow the ballast, and bring your precious cargo to the surface. You don't — she stays on the bottom forever. Your call, boss."

Alistair stared for a long moment into the grey, churning void beyond the window, watching the rain batter the glass. "Although the board of directors has on multiple occasions recommended abandoning the submerged cargo to the mercy of the implacable depths, I remain firmly convinced that the salvage must be the priority — provided the divers, duly informed of the environmental hazards, are guaranteed their exorbitant remuneration in the event of a catastrophic breakdown of the operation."

Jaxx broke into a broad grin — a white flash of teeth cutting through the dim, sullen light of the pavilion. "Now you're speaking my language, pal. Team's ready and in the water in ten minutes."

"I sincerely doubt I shall ever have occasion to speak your language, Jaxx," murmured Alistair, turning from the window as the foreman strode out of the tent into the howling storm. "But let us hope, for both our sakes, that this wretched enterprise at last bears fruit."
