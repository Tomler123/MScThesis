# Simple Harmonic Motion — Equations and Behavior

---

## 1. Key Ideas

**What is SHM?**

Simple harmonic motion is oscillatory motion produced by a special kind of restoring force: one that is always directed *toward* the equilibrium position and whose magnitude is *proportional* to the displacement from it. The classic example is a mass on a spring.

**The restoring force (Hooke's Law):**

$$F = -kx$$

Here $k > 0$ is the spring constant (stiffness) and $x$ is displacement from equilibrium. The minus sign is essential — when the block is displaced to the right ($x > 0$), the force pulls it left, and vice versa. The force always fights the displacement.

**The equation of motion:**

Applying Newton's second law $F = ma = m\ddot{x}$:

$$m\ddot{x} = -kx \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \ddot{x} = -\frac{k}{m}x$$

This is a second-order linear ODE. Its defining feature: the second derivative of $x$ is proportional to $-x$ itself.

**General solution:**

$$x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi), \qquad \omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}$$

**Definitions of each quantity:**

| Symbol | Name | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| $A$ | Amplitude | Maximum displacement from equilibrium (meters) |
| $\omega$ | Angular frequency | How fast the oscillation cycles, in radians per second |
| $T = 2\pi/\omega$ | Period | Time for one complete cycle (seconds) |
| $\phi$ | Phase constant | Sets the initial position and velocity at $t = 0$ |

**Why does this solution work?** Differentiate $x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)$ twice: $\ddot{x} = -\omega^2 A\cos(\omega t + \phi) = -\omega^2 x$. Comparing with $\ddot{x} = -(k/m)x$, you can see immediately that $\omega^2 = k/m$, confirming the solution.

## 2. Worked Example

**Setup:** $m = 0.5\ \text{kg}$, $k = 8\ \text{N/m}$, $x_0 = 0.1\ \text{m}$, released from rest ($v_0 = 0$).

**Step 1 — Angular frequency:**

$$\omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{8}{0.5}} = \sqrt{16} = 4\ \text{rad/s}$$

**Step 2 — Period:**

$$T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} = \frac{2\pi}{4} = \frac{\pi}{2} \approx 1.57\ \text{s}$$

**Step 3 — Apply initial conditions to find $A$ and $\phi$:**

The general solution is $x(t) = A\cos(4t + \phi)$. Its derivative (velocity) is $v(t) = -4A\sin(4t + \phi)$.

At $t = 0$: $x(0) = A\cos\phi = 0.1$ and $v(0) = -4A\sin\phi = 0$.

From the velocity condition: $\sin\phi = 0$, so $\phi = 0$ (or $\pi$, but $\phi = 0$ gives positive displacement, consistent with $x_0 > 0$).

Substituting back: $A\cos(0) = A = 0.1\ \text{m}$.

**Explicit solution:**

$$\boxed{x(t) = 0.1\cos(4t)\ \text{m}}$$

The block oscillates between $-0.1\ \text{m}$ and $+0.1\ \text{m}$ with a period of $\approx 1.57\ \text{s}$. Here is an interactive visualization:Use the sliders to explore how changing $k$, $m$, or $A$ affects the motion. Notice that $T$ depends only on $k$ and $m$ — not on amplitude.

## 3. Practice Exercises

---

**Exercise 1.** A block of mass $m = 2\ \text{kg}$ is connected to a spring with $k = 18\ \text{N/m}$. The block is displaced $0.3\ \text{m}$ from equilibrium and released from rest. Find $\omega$, $T$, and write $x(t)$.

**Solution:**

$$\omega = \sqrt{\frac{18}{2}} = \sqrt{9} = 3\ \text{rad/s}$$

$$T = \frac{2\pi}{3} \approx 2.09\ \text{s}$$

Initial conditions at $t = 0$: $x(0) = 0.3\ \text{m}$ and $v(0) = 0$.

Following the same reasoning as the worked example, $\phi = 0$ and $A = 0.3\ \text{m}$.

$$\boxed{x(t) = 0.3\cos(3t)\ \text{m}}$$

---

**Exercise 2.** A $0.4\ \text{kg}$ block on a frictionless surface is attached to a spring ($k = 10\ \text{N/m}$). At $t = 0$, the block is at the equilibrium position ($x = 0$) and moving in the positive direction with speed $v_0 = 0.5\ \text{m/s}$. Find $\omega$, $T$, $A$, and write $x(t)$.

**Solution:**

$$\omega = \sqrt{\frac{10}{0.4}} = \sqrt{25} = 5\ \text{rad/s}$$

$$T = \frac{2\pi}{5} \approx 1.26\ \text{s}$$

Now apply initial conditions to the general form $x(t) = A\cos(5t + \phi)$, with $v(t) = -5A\sin(5t + \phi)$:

$$x(0) = A\cos\phi = 0$$
$$v(0) = -5A\sin\phi = +0.5$$

From the first equation: $\cos\phi = 0$, so $\phi = \pm\pi/2$.

Substituting $\phi = -\pi/2$ into the velocity equation: $-5A\sin(-\pi/2) = 5A = 0.5$, giving $A = 0.1\ \text{m}$. ✓

(Checking $\phi = +\pi/2$ gives $-5A = 0.5$, a negative amplitude — unphysical by convention.)

$$\boxed{x(t) = 0.1\cos\!\left(5t - \frac{\pi}{2}\right)\ \text{m}}$$

This is equivalently written as $x(t) = 0.1\sin(5t)\ \text{m}$, which makes physical sense: starting at zero and immediately moving in the positive direction is exactly what a sine function does. The amplitude is $A = 0.1\ \text{m}$ — determined entirely by the initial *kinetic* energy, since the block starts with no potential energy stored.
